### Q1. What is Micro-Environment?

**Micro-Environment** = The forces **close to the company** that directly affect its ability to serve customers. The company has **some control/influence** over these forces.

**6 Actors in the Micro Environment:**
1. **The Company** (internal departments — marketing, finance, R&D, production, HR)
2. **Suppliers** — Provide raw materials; disruptions affect product quality
3. **Marketing Intermediaries** — Wholesalers, retailers, logistics firms
4. **Customers** — Consumer markets, business markets, reseller markets
5. **Competitors** — Must offer better value than rivals
6. **Publics** — Financial, media, government, local community publics

> 🧠 **Trick:** Micro = **"My world"** — actors close to and directly affecting the business

### Q2. What is Market Positioning?

**Philip Kotler:** *"Positioning is the act of designing the company's offering and image to occupy a **distinctive place** in the target market's mind."*

**Al Ries & Jack Trout:** *"Positioning is not what you do to a product. It is what you do to the **mind** of the prospect."*

**In simple words:** Positioning = Making your brand **own a specific word or concept** in the customer's mind compared to competitors.

| Brand | Position Owned |
|---|---|
| Volvo | Safety |
| Nike | Performance |
| Colgate | Cavity Protection |
| Amul | Taste of India |

> **Tools:** Perceptual maps, USP (Unique Selling Proposition), brand messaging

### Q3. Write TWO characteristics of Consumer Behaviour.

**1. Complex and Dynamic Process**
- Consumer behaviour involves a complex sequence of psychological processes — need recognition, information search, evaluation, decision, and post-purchase evaluation.
- It continuously changes based on income, lifestyle, trends, and social influences.

**2. Influenced by Multiple Factors**
- Consumer buying behaviour is NOT just rational — it is influenced by **Cultural** (values, subculture), **Social** (family, reference groups), **Personal** (age, income, lifestyle), and **Psychological** (motivation, perception, attitude) factors simultaneously.

### Q4. What is an Augmented Product?

**Augmented Product** = The **4th level** of Kotler's Five Levels of Product — additional features, services, and benefits **beyond what the customer normally expects** that distinguish the product and add extra value.

**Examples of Augmentation:**
- Free home delivery and installation
- Extended warranty beyond standard period
- 24x7 customer support hotline
- Free product training/tutorials
- Easy returns and exchange policy

**Example:** You buy a laptop (expected product = working laptop, charger, basic warranty). Augmented = Free 2-year extended warranty + free anti-virus software + free tech support calls.

> 🧠 **Trick:** Augmented = **"More than you expected"** — extras that wow the customer

### Q5. What is a Trademark?

**AMA Definition:** A trademark is a **word, name, symbol, or device legally registered by a manufacturer/seller** to distinguish their goods from others and prevent unauthorised use.

**Key Points:**
- It is the part of a brand that has **legal protection**
- Denoted by **® symbol** (Registered Trademark) or **™ symbol** (unregistered but claimed)
- Gives the owner the **exclusive legal right** to use it
- Protects against competitors using similar names/symbols

**Example:** Coca-Cola®, McDonald's® golden arches, Nike® swoosh logo

> 🧠 **Trick:** Trademark = Brand + **Legal Protection**

### Q6. What are Channels of Distribution?

**Philip Kotler:** *"A distribution channel is a set of interdependent organisations that help make a product or service available for use or consumption by the consumer or business user."*

**Simply:** The **path/route** goods travel from producer to final consumer — including all wholesalers, retailers, and agents.

**Channel Levels:**
- **Level 0:** Producer → Consumer (Direct)
- **Level 1:** Producer → Retailer → Consumer
- **Level 2:** Producer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer
- **Level 3:** Producer → Agent → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer

### Q7. State TWO Factors which Affect the Price of a Product.

**1. Cost of Production (Internal Factor)**
- Cost sets the **FLOOR (minimum price)** — the company must at minimum recover all its costs.
- If production cost is high, selling price must be higher; if costs fall, price can be reduced.
- Formula: Selling Price ≥ Fixed Cost + Variable Cost per unit.

**2. Competition (External Factor)**
- In competitive markets, rival companies' prices strongly constrain what price you can charge.
- If competitors price lower, customers may switch unless your product offers superior value.
- Example: When Jio cut mobile data prices to ₹0, all other telecom companies were forced to drastically reduce their prices.

### Q8. Define Green Marketing.

**Green Marketing** = Developing, promoting, pricing, and distributing products that are **environmentally responsible** and cause minimal harm to the environment.

**Also called:** Eco-marketing | Environmental marketing | Sustainable marketing

**Key Elements:**
- Products made with eco-friendly, recyclable materials
- Environmentally safe manufacturing
- Honest environmental claims (avoid "greenwashing" — false environmental claims)
- Sustainable packaging

**Examples:** Tesla electric vehicles | Bamboo products | ITC eco-friendly packaging | Organic food brands

> 🧠 **Trick:** Green Marketing = "Marketing that is GOOD for the planet"

### Q9. What is Personal Selling?

**Personal Selling** = A **direct, face-to-face interaction** between a company's salesperson and a potential customer with the aim of making a sale.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Two-way communication** — immediate interaction and feedback
- **Most persuasive** of all promotional tools — can address individual concerns
- **Most expensive** per contact — salesperson time and travel costs are high
- Message can be **tailored** to each individual prospect

**Examples:**
- Insurance agent visiting your home to explain a policy
- Pharmaceutical medical representative (MR) visiting doctors
- Car showroom salesperson guiding a test drive

---

### Q10. What is Rural Marketing?

**Rural Marketing** = All marketing activities that promote the **flow of goods and services from urban/manufacturing centres to rural areas** and vice versa — targeting rural consumers.

**Characteristics of Rural Markets:**
- Large but geographically dispersed population
- Lower income levels → price sensitivity is high
- Strong cultural traditions and local preferences
- Limited infrastructure (roads, internet, electricity)
- Growing aspirations and rising income

**Challenges:** Poor connectivity | Low literacy | Limited distribution reach | Seasonal income

**Examples:**
- HUL's sachet strategy (small, affordable packs) for rural consumers
- Chaupal Sagar (ITC e-Choupal) for rural product distribution
- Godrej's ChotuKool — low-cost refrigerator for rural markets

### Q1. Discuss the Different Components of Macro Environment.

**Macro Environment** = The large societal forces that affect the entire micro environment. Largely **uncontrollable** — company must monitor and adapt.

**PESTLE Framework:**

**1. Political/Legal Environment**
- Government policies, laws, regulations affecting business
- Tax laws (GST), trade policies, consumer protection laws, advertising restrictions
- Example: India banning single-use plastics forced companies to change packaging

**2. Economic Environment**
- Economic conditions affecting purchasing power
- GDP growth, inflation, interest rates, unemployment, income distribution
- Example: During COVID recession, consumers became highly price-sensitive

**3. Socio-Cultural Environment**
- Social values, demographics, lifestyle trends, cultural norms
- Population growth, health consciousness, urbanisation
- Example: Growing health awareness → boom in organic food and fitness industry

**4. Technological Environment**
- Fastest-changing force — creates new opportunities and threats
- AI, e-commerce, 5G, automation, data analytics, social media
- Example: E-commerce opened entirely new distribution channels; AI enables personalisation

**5. Legal/Ecological (Natural) Environment**
- Environmental concerns, sustainability, climate change
- Raw material scarcity, pollution regulations, green movement
- Example: Companies redesigning packaging; electric vehicle mandates by governments

**6. Competitive Environment**
- Porter's 5 Forces — threat of new entrants, bargaining power of buyers/suppliers, substitutes, rivalry
- Example: Reliance Jio's entry created massive competitive pressure on Airtel and Vodafone

### Q2. Explain the Consumer Buying Decision Process.

*(See 2024 Paper Section 3, Q1 — identical question. Same answer applies.)*

**5 Stages:**

**Stage 1: Need/Problem Recognition**
- Gap between current and desired state triggers the process
- Internal (hunger, broken product) or External stimulus (ad, friend)

**Stage 2: Information Search**
- Internal search (memory) + External search (Google, friends, reviews, ads)
- Creates **awareness set** then narrows to **evoked set**

**Stage 3: Evaluation of Alternatives**
- Compares options on: Price | Quality | Brand | Features | After-sales
- **Evoked set** = final shortlist of acceptable brands

**Stage 4: Purchase Decision**
- Selects one brand
- Can be influenced at last moment by availability, in-store deals, or friend's advice

**Stage 5: Post-Purchase Evaluation**
- Performance vs. Expectations:
  - ≥ Expectations → Satisfied → Repeat purchase + Word-of-mouth
  - < Expectations → Dissatisfied → Cognitive Dissonance → Complaint or switch

> 🧠 **Trick:** **"NI-EPP"** → Need → Info → Evaluate → Purchase → Post-purchase

### Q4. Discuss the Phases of Consumer Adoption Process.

**Consumer Adoption Process** = The mental stages a consumer goes through in deciding to adopt (regularly use) a **new product/innovation.**

**Also called: Innovation Adoption Process (Everett Rogers)**

**5 Phases:**

**Phase 1: AWARENESS**
- Consumer becomes aware the new product exists, but has NO information about it
- Still unfamiliar — just knows the name
- **Marketer's Role:** Mass advertising to create awareness

**Phase 2: INTEREST**
- Consumer develops interest and actively **seeks information** about the new product
- Asks questions, reads reviews, looks up details
- **Marketer's Role:** Provide detailed product information through all channels

**Phase 3: EVALUATION**
- Consumer mentally **evaluates** whether the product is worth trying
- Compares benefits vs risks; considers cost, convenience, relevance to their needs
- **Marketer's Role:** Provide testimonials, demonstrations, expert endorsements to help evaluation

**Phase 4: TRIAL**
- Consumer tries the product on a **small scale** to experience it firsthand
- **Marketer's Role:** Offer free samples, trial packs, money-back guarantees, demos

**Phase 5: ADOPTION**
- If trial experience is positive → consumer decides to **regularly adopt and use** the product
- Becomes a loyal/repeat buyer
- **Marketer's Role:** Reward loyalty, ensure product availability, provide after-sales support

**Adopter Categories (Rogers):**

| Category | % of Market | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Innovators | 2.5% | Tech-enthusiasts; first to try anything new |
| Early Adopters | 13.5% | Opinion leaders; respected; adopt early |
| Early Majority | 34% | Deliberate but ahead of average person |
| Late Majority | 34% | Skeptical; adopt only after most others have |
| Laggards | 16% | Tradition-bound; last to adopt |

> 🧠 **Trick:** **A-I-E-T-A** → Awareness → Interest → Evaluation → Trial → Adoption

### Q5. What are the Functions of a Middleman? Explain.

**Middlemen** (Marketing Intermediaries) = Individuals or organisations that link producers to consumers — wholesalers, retailers, agents, brokers.

**8 Key Functions:**

1. **Bulk Breaking** — Buy in large quantities from producers; sell in smaller lots to retailers/consumers; makes products affordable for small buyers

2. **Warehousing and Storage** — Store goods until needed; ensures continuous supply even when production is seasonal

3. **Transportation** — Move goods from producers' locations to consumers' locations across geographies

4. **Financing** — Provide credit to retailers (buy now, pay later); some pay producers in advance; eases cash flow

5. **Risk Bearing** — Hold inventory; bear risk of price changes, damage, theft, and unsold stock

6. **Market Information** — Gather and pass information about consumer preferences, market trends, and competitor activities back to producers

7. **Promotion** — Assist in promoting products through in-store displays, local advertising, and point-of-sale materials

8. **Standardisation and Grading** — Sort and grade products by quality, size, weight — makes buying/selling simpler

### Q1. Describe the Evolution of Marketing Concept.

*(Identical to 2024 Paper Section 4, Q — same as 5 Eras. Use Part 1 Long Q1 answer.)*

**Marketing Evolution** = How the business philosophy toward customers changed over time.

**Era 1: Production Era (Pre-1920s)**
- **Belief:** "If we make it, it will sell" | **Focus:** Maximise production efficiency
- **Reason:** Supply < Demand — everything manufactured got sold
- **Limitation:** Ignored what customers wanted
- **Example:** Henry Ford — "Any colour as long as it's black"

**Era 2: Product Era (1920s–30s)**
- **Belief:** "Best quality product automatically sells"
- **Focus:** Product improvement and engineering excellence
- **Limitation:** Marketing Myopia — too product-focused, missed customer's real need
- **Example:** Buggy whip makers improving product while customers switched to cars

**Era 3: Selling/Sales Era (1930s–50s)**
- **Belief:** "Customers won't buy unless we push them"
- **Reason:** Great Depression — surplus goods, too few buyers
- **Focus:** Aggressive advertising, personal selling, promotions
- **Limitation:** Damaged trust; temporary sales, no relationships
- **Example:** High-pressure door-to-door salespeople

**Era 4: Marketing/Customer Era (1950s–90s)**
- **Belief:** "Find what customers want, then make it"
- **The Big Shift:** "Sell what we make" → **"Make what we can sell"**
- **3 Pillars:** Customer Orientation | Integrated Marketing | Profitability
- **Example:** P&G conducts consumer research BEFORE developing any product

**Era 5: Societal/Holistic Era (1990s–Now)**
- **Belief:** "Satisfy customers AND care for society AND planet"
- **Balances:** Customer Wants + Company Profits + Society's Welfare
- **Includes:** CSR, Green Marketing, Digital Marketing, Relationship Marketing
- **Example:** Tata's CSR | Eco-friendly packaging | Cause-related marketing

> 🧠 **Trick:** **"People Prefer Selling More Sensibly"** (P→P→S→M→S)

### Q2. Discuss the Factors that Influence Consumer Behaviour.

*(Identical to 2024 Paper Section 4, Q4 — same answer. Use CSPP Framework below)*

Consumer behaviour is influenced by **4 major factors:**

**A. CULTURAL FACTORS** *(Deepest influence)*

1. **Culture** — Core values, perceptions, and preferences learned from family and society
   - Example: McDonald's adapted menu for India (no beef) — McAloo Tikki burger

2. **Subculture** — Nationality, religion, region within culture
   - Example: South Indian food preferences (rice-based) differ from North Indian (wheat-based)

3. **Social Class** — Position in society determined by income, occupation, education
   - Example: Upper class buys luxury brands; middle class prefers value brands

**B. SOCIAL FACTORS**

1. **Reference Groups** — Groups that influence attitudes and behaviour
   - Aspirational groups: Celebrities endorse brands because consumers want to be like them
   - Example: Virat Kohli endorsing Puma

2. **Family** — Most important reference group for purchasing
   - Example: Parents heavily influence children's brand preferences; spouse influences household purchases

3. **Roles and Status** — A CEO buys a BMW to signal status; a student buys a budget phone

**C. PERSONAL FACTORS**

1. **Age and Life-Cycle Stage** — Infants need baby food; young adults buy adventure products; seniors need health products

2. **Occupation and Income** — Manual worker vs manager have very different product and brand choices

3. **Lifestyle** — Health-conscious lifestyle → organic food, gym memberships, sports wear

4. **Personality and Self-Concept** — Adventurous personality → Royal Enfield, Red Bull; conservative → traditional brands

**D. PSYCHOLOGICAL FACTORS**

1. **Motivation (Maslow's Hierarchy)**
   - Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-Actualisation
   - Example: Buying a Rolex = esteem/status need being fulfilled

2. **Perception** — How a person selects, organises, and interprets information
   - Selective Attention | Selective Distortion | Selective Retention
   - Two people see the same ad differently based on their own perceptions

3. **Learning** — Experience changes behaviour
   - Positive experience with Colgate → continue buying it (conditioned response)

4. **Beliefs and Attitudes**
   - Belief: "Apple makes reliable phones" → favourable attitude → purchase
   - Attitudes are hard to change; marketers try to align with existing attitudes

> 🧠 **Trick:** **"CSPP"** → Cultural (deepest), Social, Personal, Psychological

### Q3. What is a Product? Explain the Process of New Product Development.

**PRODUCT DEFINITION:**

**Philip Kotler:** *"A product is anything that can be offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use or consumption that might satisfy a want or need."*

Product includes: **Physical Goods | Services | Ideas | Persons | Places | Experiences**

**5 Levels of Product (Kotler):**

| Level | Name | Description |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Core Product | Fundamental benefit (drill → HOLES) |
| 2 | Actual Product | Brand, features, quality, design, packaging |
| 3 | Expected Product | What customers normally expect |
| 4 | Augmented Product | Extra benefits — warranty, delivery, support |
| 5 | Potential Product | All future improvements |

---

**NEW PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT (NPD) PROCESS — 8 Stages:**

**Stage 1: Idea Generation**
- Find new ideas from internal (R&D, employees) + external (customers, competitors) sources
- Techniques: Brainstorming, focus groups, customer surveys

**Stage 2: Idea Screening**
- Filter ideas — keep good, reject bad
- **Drop Error:** Good idea rejected | **Go Error:** Bad idea approved (both costly!)

**Stage 3: Concept Development and Testing**
- Develop detailed product concept from customer's viewpoint
- Test with target consumers: "Would you buy this?"

**Stage 4: Marketing Strategy Development**
- Define target market, positioning, pricing, distribution, and promotion plan

**Stage 5: Business Analysis**
- Estimate sales, costs, break-even point, projected profit
- If financially attractive → proceed

**Stage 6: Product Development (Prototype)**
- R&D builds the physical prototype
- ⚠️ Most expensive stage — if prototype fails, huge investment is lost
- Consumer tests on prototype product

**Stage 7: Test Marketing**
- Launch in 1–2 representative markets
- Collect real sales data; fine-tune marketing mix
- Advantage: Reduces risk | Disadvantage: Alerts competitors; time-consuming

**Stage 8: Commercialisation (Full Launch)**
- Full national/international launch
- Decide: WHEN + WHERE + TO WHOM + HOW to launch

> 🧠 **Trick (8 stages in order):** Idea → Screen → Concept → Strategy → Business → Prototype → Test → Launch

### Q4. Discuss the Various Types of Retailing.

**Retailing** = All activities of selling goods or services directly to **final consumers** for personal, non-business use.

**Philip Kotler:** *"Retailing includes all activities involved in selling goods or services directly to final consumers for personal, non-business use."*

**TYPES OF RETAILING:**

**A. STORE-BASED RETAILING:**

**1. Departmental Stores**
- Very large stores with **many departments** under one roof — clothing, electronics, food, furniture
- Wide variety; medium to high prices; full customer service
- Example: **Shoppers Stop, Lifestyle, Westside**

**2. Supermarkets / Hypermarkets**
- Large, **self-service** stores selling food + household goods at low prices
- High volume, low margin; appeals to bulk-buying consumers
- Example: **Big Bazaar, DMart, Spencer's** | Supermarkets: **Foodworld**

**3. Specialty Stores**
- Focus on **one specific product category** with deep assortment and expert staff
- Example: **Tanishq** (jewellery) | **Croma** (electronics) | **Bata** (footwear) | **Crossword** (books)

**4. Convenience Stores**
- Small stores in **residential areas**, open long hours, selling daily necessities
- High prices but time-saving convenience
- Example: **Local kirana shops, 7-Eleven (internationally)**

**5. Discount Stores**
- Sell at **below-normal prices** by keeping costs low (minimal services, simple decor)
- Example: **Big Bazaar (discount section), Price Club (USA), Costco**

**6. Off-Price Retailers**
- Sell branded goods at below regular prices — manufacturer surplus or end-of-season stock
- Example: **Brand Factory, TK Maxx (UK)**

**B. NON-STORE RETAILING:**

**7. Direct Selling**
- Salesperson visits consumer's home or office to sell
- Example: Amway, Tupperware, Avon, insurance agents

**8. Mail-Order / Catalogue Retailing**
- Consumer orders from printed catalogue by post or phone
- Example: Book clubs, traditional insurance companies

**9. Vending Machines**
- Automated machines dispensing products on inserting coins/card
- Example: Beverage vending machines in offices, airports, stations

**10. Online / E-Commerce Retailing**
- Buying and selling through the internet — fastest growing form of retail
- Example: **Amazon, Flipkart, Myntra, Nykaa**

**C. CORPORATE RETAIL FORMATS:**

**11. Franchising**
- Retailer gets right to use franchisor's brand, system, and support for a fee
- Example: **McDonald's, Domino's, KFC, Subway, DTDC courier**

**12. Superstore / Megastore**
- Extremely large stores selling both food and non-food items — combines supermarket + specialty
- Example: **Walmart Supercenter, Hypercity**
