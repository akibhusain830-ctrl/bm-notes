## 🧠 SHORT QUESTIONS (2–3 Marks)

### Q1. ⭐⭐⭐ Define Price in marketing.

**Philip Kotler:** *"Price is the amount of money charged for a product or service, or the sum of values that consumers exchange for the benefits of having or using the product."*

**Key fact:** Price is the **ONLY element of the marketing mix that generates REVENUE.** All other Ps (Product, Place, Promotion) generate **costs.**

---

### Q2. ⭐⭐⭐ Why is pricing uniquely significant vs. the other 3 Ps?

1. **Only Revenue Generator** — generates revenue; others generate costs
2. **Most Flexible Tool** — can be changed overnight (unlike product design)
3. **Communicates Positioning** — High price = premium | Low price = value
4. **Directly Affects Demand** — Law of Demand
5. **Competitive Weapon** — price cuts gain market share

---

### Q3. ⭐⭐⭐ Name 3 Internal + 3 External factors affecting pricing.

**Internal (Controllable):**
1. **Cost of Production** — sets the FLOOR (minimum price)
2. **Marketing Objectives** — survival/profit/market share goal
3. **Brand Strength/Differentiation** — unique products charge more

**External (Less Controllable):**
1. **Demand/Perceived Value** — sets the CEILING (maximum price)
2. **Competition** — rival prices constrain your own
3. **Government Regulations** — MRP, price controls, GST

---

### Q4. ⭐⭐ What is Cost-Plus (Mark-Up) Pricing?

**Definition:** Add a standard % profit margin (mark-up) on top of the cost of production.

**Formula:**
```
Selling Price = Cost + (Mark-up % × Cost)
Example: Cost = ₹100 | Mark-up = 30% | Selling Price = ₹130
```

- **Advantage:** Simple; ensures cost recovery
- **Disadvantage:** Ignores what customers value and what competitors charge

---

### Q5. ⭐⭐⭐ Market Skimming vs Penetration Pricing?

| Aspect | Market Skimming | Penetration Pricing |
|---|---|---|
| **Initial Price** | **HIGH** | **LOW** |
| **Goal** | Skim maximum revenue from early adopters | Gain market share quickly |
| **Best When** | Innovative product; loyal early adopters | Price-sensitive market; rapid adoption needed |
| **Example** | New iPhone at ₹1,20,000 | Jio launched at ₹0 to capture market |

> 🧠 **Trick:** Skimming = **"Skim the cream"** (charge premium buyers first) | Penetration = **"Penetrate deep"** (go low to get in everywhere)

---

### Q6. ⭐⭐ What is Psychological Pricing? Give an example.

**Definition:** Pricing that uses **consumer psychology** — making prices "feel" cheaper than they are.

**How it works:** Prices ending in 9 feel like they belong to a lower category in the buyer's mind.

**Examples:**
- ₹999 instead of ₹1,000
- ₹4,999 instead of ₹5,000
- "Buy 2 Get 1 Free" (feels better than 33% off, even though it's the same)

> 🧠 **Trick:** **"The 9-Trick"** — prices ending in 9 always feel one digit cheaper

---

### Q7. ⭐⭐ Define Price Elasticity of Demand (PED).

**Definition:** Measures how **sensitive demand is to a change in price.**

**Formula:**
```
PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded ÷ % Change in Price
```

| PED | Type | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **> 1** | Elastic | Big drop in demand if price rises | Luxury goods, optional items |
| **< 1** | Inelastic | Small drop in demand if price rises | Essential goods, medicines, petrol |
| **= 1** | Unit Elastic | Equal % change in demand and price | — |

**Marketing Impact:**
- Elastic demand → Lower price → More buyers → Revenue rises
- Inelastic demand → Higher price → Demand barely falls → Revenue rises

> 🧠 **Trick:** **Elastic = Sensitive** (stretches with price change) | **Inelastic = NOT Sensitive** (people buy anyway)

---

### Q8. ⭐⭐ When should a company use Premium Pricing?

A company should use **Premium (Prestige) Pricing** when:
1. The brand has a **strong luxury image** (Rolex, Gucci, Mercedes)
2. Target segment is **status-conscious** and sees price as a quality signal
3. Product genuinely offers **superior quality or exclusivity**
4. Lowering price would **damage the brand's image**
5. Company wants to position at the **top end** of the market

### Q1. ⭐⭐⭐ Explain the Internal and External Factors Affecting Pricing.

**INTERNAL FACTORS (Controllable by company):**

| Factor | How It Affects Pricing |
|---|---|
| **Cost of Production** | Sets the FLOOR — price must cover costs; selling below cost = loss |
| **Marketing Objectives** | Survival → low price | Market share → penetration | Profit max → high price |
| **Marketing Mix** | Premium product + luxury channels → high price expected |
| **Product Differentiation** | Unique products with no substitutes → can charge more |
| **Brand Strength** | Strong brand = higher price acceptable (brand equity) |
| **Organisational Factors** | Who has pricing authority in the company |

**EXTERNAL FACTORS (Less controllable):**

| Factor | How It Affects Pricing |
|---|---|
| **Demand / Perceived Value** | Sets the CEILING — max price customer will pay |
| **Competition** | Rivals' prices constrain your pricing decisions |
| **Economic Conditions** | Recession → customers price-sensitive → pressure to lower prices |
| **Government Regulations** | MRP, price controls, GST, anti-dumping laws |
| **Distribution Channel** | Intermediary margins must be included in price |
| **Social Considerations** | Essential goods must be priced fairly (public pressure) |

> 🧠 **Summary:** **Internal = FLOOR factors** | **External = CEILING factors**

### Q2. ⭐⭐ Cost-Based vs Value-Based Pricing.

**COST-BASED PRICING (Inside-Out approach)**
```
PRODUCT → COST → PRICE → VALUE → CUSTOMER
```
- Start with **cost of production** → add markup → selling price
- Methods: Cost-Plus Pricing | Break-Even Pricing
- **Example:** Cost ₹100 + 30% markup = Selling Price ₹130
- **Advantage:** Simple, ensures cost recovery
- **Disadvantage:** Ignores what customers actually value; ignores competition

**VALUE-BASED PRICING (Outside-In approach)**
```
CUSTOMER → VALUE → PRICE → COST → PRODUCT
```
- Start with **what the customer thinks it's worth** → set price accordingly → build product/cost to match
- **Example:** Luxury brands price based on perceived prestige, NOT cost of materials
- **Advantage:** Captures more profit when customers value the product highly
- **Disadvantage:** Hard to measure perceived value accurately

> 🧠 **Key Difference:**
> - Cost-based: "How much does it **cost to make**?" → set price
> - Value-based: "How much is it **worth to the customer**?" → set price

### Q3. ⭐⭐⭐ Describe 5 Major Pricing Strategies with Examples.

**1. 🚀 Market Skimming**
- High initial price → lower gradually over time
- **Logic:** Capture revenue from early adopters (less price-sensitive) before mass market
- **Best when:** Innovative product; strong brand; early adopters will pay premium
- **Example:** New iPhone launches at ₹1,20,000 → price drops over months

**2. 📉 Penetration Pricing**
- Very low initial price → raise later after capturing market share
- **Logic:** Low price builds mass customer base quickly; discourages new competitors
- **Best when:** Price-sensitive market; need rapid adoption
- **Example:** Reliance Jio launched at ₹0 to acquire millions of subscribers instantly

**3. 🧠 Psychological Pricing**
- Price to exploit consumer psychology
- **Logic:** Certain prices "feel" significantly cheaper than they are
- **Best when:** Consumer emotion drives purchase
- **Example:** ₹999 instead of ₹1,000 | "Buy 2 Get 1 Free"

**4. 📦 Bundle Pricing**
- Sell multiple products together at one combined price (lower than buying separately)
- **Logic:** Increases perceived value; moves slow-selling products
- **Best when:** Company has complementary products
- **Example:** McDonald's value meal (burger + fries + drink = ₹99) | Telecom bundle plans

**5. 🎉 Promotional Pricing**
- Temporarily reduce price to stimulate sales
- **Logic:** Creates urgency; clears excess inventory; attracts price-sensitive shoppers
- **Best when:** Slow seasons, clearance sales, festivals, new launches
- **Example:** Flipkart Big Billion Day | Diwali offers | End-of-Season sales

*Other strategies:* Premium Pricing | Economy Pricing | Geographic Pricing | Discriminatory Pricing

> 🧠 **Trick:** **"S-P-P-B-P"** → Skimming, Penetration, Psychological, Bundle, Promotional

### Q4. ⭐⭐ How does Price Elasticity of Demand influence pricing strategy?

**PED = % Change in Demand ÷ % Change in Price**

**Case 1: Elastic Demand (PED > 1)**
- Demand is **very sensitive to price changes**
- Products: Luxury goods, optional items, branded goods with substitutes
- **Strategy:** Keep prices competitive; use promotions
- **Revenue Impact:** ↑ Price → demand falls a lot → **Revenue FALLS** | ↓ Price → demand rises a lot → **Revenue RISES**
- **Example:** Premium restaurant — a 20% price hike drives customers to cheaper alternatives

**Case 2: Inelastic Demand (PED < 1)**
- Demand is **NOT very sensitive to price changes**
- Products: Essential goods, medicines, petrol, addictive products
- **Strategy:** Can raise price to increase revenue
- **Revenue Impact:** ↑ Price → demand barely falls → **Revenue RISES**
- **Example:** Insulin for diabetic patients — even if price doubles, patients must still buy

| PED Situation | Strategy | Revenue Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Elastic demand, want more revenue | **Lower the price** | More volume = more revenue |
| Inelastic demand, want more revenue | **Raise the price** | Demand won't fall much |

---

### Q5. ⭐⭐ When does a company initiate Price Cuts or Price Increases?

**📉 PRICE CUTS — When:**
1. **Excess Production Capacity** — Need to increase volume to utilise idle capacity
2. **Declining Market Share** — Losing customers to cheaper competitors
3. **Economic Recession** — Consumers become price-sensitive
4. **Competitive Pressure** — Match a competitor's price cut to retain customers

**📈 PRICE INCREASES — When:**
1. **Cost Inflation** — Rising raw materials, labour, energy costs
2. **Excess Demand** — Demand exceeds supply capacity
3. **Improving Profitability** — Company prioritises margins over market share

**🔄 Responding to Competitor Price Changes:**

| Situation | Best Response |
|---|---|
| Competitor cuts price in price-sensitive market | Match the cut to defend market share |
| Competitor cuts price but your brand is differentiated | Maintain price; increase advertising to reinforce value |
| Competitor raises price | Also raise price to improve margins |
| Competitor enters with very low price | Launch a separate low-price "fighter brand" |
