# 📌 CHAPTER 1: MARKETING — NATURE, SCOPE AND IMPORTANCE

---

## 🔷 What is Marketing?

Marketing is far more than just selling or advertising. It is a **comprehensive, customer-centred business process** that begins long before a product is made and continues long after it is sold.

In simple terms, **Marketing** is the process of **identifying customer needs and wants, creating products and services that satisfy those needs, and delivering them to customers profitably** — while building long-term relationships with those customers.

---

## 🔷 Key Definitions of Marketing

> **Philip Kotler (Father of Modern Marketing):**
> *"Marketing is a social and managerial process by which individuals and groups obtain what they need and want through creating, offering and exchanging products of value with others."*

> **American Marketing Association (AMA), 2013:**
> *"Marketing is the activity, set of institutions, and processes for creating, communicating, delivering, and exchanging offerings that have value for customers, clients, partners, and society at large."*

> **William J. Stanton:**
> *"Marketing is a total system of business activities designed to plan, price, promote and distribute want-satisfying products to target markets to achieve organisational objectives."*

> **Peter Drucker:**
> *"The aim of marketing is to know and understand the customer so well that the product or service fits him and sells itself."*

> **CIM (Chartered Institute of Marketing):**
> *"Marketing is the management process responsible for identifying, anticipating and satisfying customer requirements profitably."*

---

## 🔷 Core Concepts of Marketing

### 1. 🧩 Needs, Wants, and Demands

| Concept | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Need** | A basic human requirement — something essential for survival or well-being. Needs are NOT created by marketers; they are inherent. | Need for food, shelter, clothing, safety, belonging |
| **Want** | A specific way a need is satisfied, shaped by culture, personality, and individual preference. Wants ARE influenced by marketing. | Need for food → Want for a Domino's pizza |
| **Demand** | A want backed by purchasing power — the willingness AND ability to buy | Want for a BMW → Demand only if you can afford it |

> **Key Marketing Insight:** Marketers do not create needs. They influence *wants* and try to convert wants into *demands* by making products attractive, accessible, and affordable.

---

### 2. 💎 Value, Satisfaction, and Quality

**Customer Value** = The benefit a customer gets from a product MINUS the cost (money, time, effort) of getting it.

```
Customer Value = Total Benefits − Total Costs
               = (Functional + Social + Emotional benefits)
                 − (Monetary + Time + Energy + Psychological costs)
```

**Customer Satisfaction** = The degree to which a product's actual performance **meets or exceeds** the customer's expectations.

```
If Performance < Expectations → Customer is DISSATISFIED
If Performance = Expectations → Customer is SATISFIED
If Performance > Expectations → Customer is DELIGHTED
```

**Quality** = The totality of features and characteristics of a product that bear on its ability to satisfy stated or implied needs.

---

### 3. 🔄 Exchange, Transactions, and Relationships

**Exchange** is the act of obtaining a desired object from someone by **offering something in return.** It is the core of marketing.

For exchange to occur:
- At least **two parties** must be involved
- Each party must have **something of value** to offer the other
- Each party must be **capable of communication** and delivery
- Each party must be **free to accept or reject** the offer
- Each party must believe it is **appropriate and desirable** to deal with the other

**Transaction** = A completed exchange — specifying what is given, what is received, and when/where it happens.

**Relationship Marketing** = Building long-term, mutually beneficial relationships with customers (not just one-time transactions). The goal is **customer lifetime value** — the total profit a customer generates over their entire relationship with the company.

---

### 4. 🛒 Market

A **market** consists of all **actual and potential buyers** of a product — people who share a particular need or want that can be satisfied through exchange.

**Types of Markets:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Consumer Market** | Individuals and households buying for personal use | Grocery shoppers, clothing buyers |
| **Business/Industrial Market** | Organisations buying for business use (production, resale, operations) | A factory buying raw materials |
| **Reseller Market** | Intermediaries buying to resell at a profit | Wholesalers, retailers |
| **Government Market** | Government agencies buying for public services | Government buying computers for offices |
| **International Market** | Buyers in foreign countries | Export markets |

---

## 🔷 Nature of Marketing

### 1. 🎯 Customer-Oriented
Modern marketing is entirely focused on the **customer** — understanding their needs and satisfying them better than competitors. The customer is the centre of all marketing activity.

### 2. 🔄 Continuous Process
Marketing is not a one-time activity. It is a **continuous, ongoing process** — from identifying needs, creating products, promoting them, distributing them, and building ongoing relationships.

### 3. 🌐 Pervasive
Marketing is relevant to **all types of organisations** — businesses, NGOs, hospitals, educational institutions, political parties, governments — and to individuals too.

### 4. 📋 Goal-Oriented
Marketing activities are always directed toward **specific organisational goals** — whether profit, market share, brand awareness, or social impact.

### 5. 🔬 Both Science and Art
- **Science:** Uses research, data analysis, consumer psychology, statistics
- **Art:** Requires creativity, intuition, and aesthetic judgement in crafting messages and experiences

### 6. ⚙️ Integrated Function
Marketing does not work in isolation — it must be **integrated** with production, finance, HR, and all other business functions.

### 7. 🤝 Exchange-Based
At its core, marketing facilitates **voluntary exchange** — both parties must benefit for a genuine marketing relationship to exist.

### 8. 🌱 Creates Utility
Marketing creates **utility** — the ability of a product to satisfy a want or need:

| Type of Utility | Description | Created By |
|---|---|---|
| **Form Utility** | Converting raw materials into a usable product | Production (with marketing input) |
| **Place Utility** | Making the product available where customers want it | Distribution/Channels |
| **Time Utility** | Making the product available when customers need it | Inventory management |
| **Possession Utility** | Facilitating transfer of ownership | Sales process |
| **Information Utility** | Telling customers about the product | Promotion/Advertising |

## 🔷 Scope of Marketing (What Does Marketing Cover?)

Marketing covers a **wide range** of activities and applies to diverse entities:

### 1. Goods
Physical products — from consumer goods (FMCG, durables) to industrial goods (machinery, raw materials).

### 2. Services
Intangible offerings — banking, healthcare, education, hospitality, consulting.

### 3. Ideas and Causes
Social marketing promotes ideas — anti-tobacco campaigns, road safety, vaccination drives, environmental awareness.

### 4. Persons
Personal branding — politicians, celebrities, athletes market themselves.

### 5. Places
Destination marketing — "Incredible India" campaign markets India as a tourist destination.

### 6. Organisations
Institutional marketing — universities, hospitals, NGOs market their capabilities and values.

### 7. Experiences
Theme parks, concerts, adventure tourism — marketing experiences, not just products.

### 8. Information
Encyclopaedias, research reports, news platforms — marketing information as a product.

## 🔷 Importance of Marketing

### 🔹 For Business / Organisations:

**1. Generates Revenue and Profit**
Marketing is the **primary revenue-generating function** of a business. Without effective marketing, there are no customers, no sales, and no profit.

**2. Identifies and Develops Markets**
Marketing continuously identifies **new customer segments, new needs, and new geographic markets** — helping businesses grow.

**3. Creates Competitive Advantage**
Through branding, positioning, and differentiation, marketing helps businesses stand out from competitors and command **premium prices and customer loyalty.**

**4. Facilitates Product Development**
Market research (part of marketing) provides the **customer insights** needed to develop products that truly meet customer needs.

**5. Builds Brand Equity**
Marketing builds **brand awareness, trust, and loyalty** — creating brand equity (the value premium a brand commands over generic alternatives).

**6. Drives Business Growth**
By expanding market share, entering new markets, and launching new products — marketing is the **engine of business growth.**

---

### 🔹 For Consumers:

**1. Provides Information**
Marketing **informs consumers** about available products, their features, prices, and where to buy — enabling better purchasing decisions.

**2. Increases Variety and Choice**
Competition driven by marketing leads to **greater product variety** — giving consumers more choices.

**3. Improves Quality and Value**
To win customers, companies must **continuously improve quality** while offering competitive prices.

**4. Creates Satisfaction**
Good marketing ensures products genuinely **meet or exceed customer expectations** — creating satisfaction and delight.

**5. Lowers Prices Over Time**
Economies of scale achieved through marketing-driven volume growth often lead to **lower prices** for consumers over time.

---

### 🔹 For Society / Economy:

**1. Employment Generation**
Marketing activities — advertising, distribution, retail, market research — **employ millions of people.**

**2. Raises Standard of Living**
By making diverse products and services accessible and affordable, marketing **raises the standard of living** of society.

**3. Connects Producers and Consumers**
Marketing bridges the **geographical, temporal, and informational gap** between producers and consumers — making the economy function efficiently.

**4. Promotes Innovation**
Marketing incentivises **investment in R&D and innovation** — because companies that innovate gain marketing advantages.

**5. Contributes to GDP**
Marketing-intensive industries (consumer goods, services, media, retail) are **major contributors to national economic output.**

# 📌 CHAPTER 2: EVOLUTION OF MARKETING

## 🔷 How Has Marketing Evolved Over Time?

Marketing has evolved dramatically over the past 200+ years — from a simple selling function to a complex, customer-centred discipline. This evolution is captured in **5 major eras/orientations:**

---

## Era 1: 🏭 The Production Era (Pre-1920s)

**Time Period:** Late 1800s to early 1900s (Industrial Revolution era)

**Core Belief:**
> *"A good product will sell itself. The key is to produce as much as possible as cheaply as possible."*

**Key Characteristics:**
- Supply was limited; **demand exceeded supply**
- Companies focused entirely on **maximising production efficiency**
- Little attention paid to what customers actually wanted
- Distribution was the main challenge — just getting products to customers
- **"If we make it, they will buy it"** mentality

**Management Focus:** Production capacity, efficiency, cost reduction

**Example:** Henry Ford's Model T — *"You can have any colour you like, as long as it's black"* — pure production orientation.

**Limitation:** As supply caught up with demand, companies found that simply making more was not enough — customers wanted choice and value.

---

## Era 2: 🛍️ The Product Era (1920s–1930s)

**Core Belief:**
> *"Customers will buy the product with the best quality, features, and performance."*

**Key Characteristics:**
- Companies obsessed with **product quality and features**
- Belief that superior products automatically attract buyers
- Continuous **product improvement** was the priority
- Little attention to customer feedback or market needs
- **"Marketing myopia"** — focusing on the product rather than the customer's real need

**Management Focus:** Product quality, engineering excellence, features

**Classic Example:** A company that makes the "best quality" horse-drawn buggy whips — ignoring the fact that customers are switching to automobiles.

**Limitation:** Companies suffered from "marketing myopia" — they were so focused on their product that they missed the bigger picture of what customers actually needed.

> **Philip Kotler's "Marketing Myopia" (Theodore Levitt):**
> Railroad companies failed because they defined themselves as being in the "railroad business" rather than the "transportation business" — and missed the rise of cars and airlines.

---

## Era 3: 💼 The Selling/Sales Era (1930s–1950s)

**Core Belief:**
> *"Customers won't buy unless we aggressively sell to them. Push, promote, and persuade."*

**Key Characteristics:**
- The Great Depression (1930s) created surplus production — too many goods, too few buyers
- Companies turned to **aggressive selling and promotion** to move inventory
- Focus on **short-term sales** — not long-term customer satisfaction
- Heavy use of **personal selling, advertising, and promotions**
- Customer's real needs were secondary to closing the sale
- **"Caveat emptor"** (Buyer beware) philosophy

**Management Focus:** Sales force, advertising, promotions, convincing people to buy

**Example:** High-pressure car salespeople, door-to-door encyclopaedia salespeople

**Limitation:** Aggressive selling might make a one-time sale but damages **customer trust and long-term relationships.** Customer satisfaction and repeat business suffered.

---

## Era 4: 🎯 The Marketing/Customer Era (1950s–1990s)

**Core Belief:**
> *"Find out what customers need and want, then create products to satisfy those needs better than competitors."*

**Key Characteristics:**
- Fundamental shift from **"sell what we make"** to **"make what customers want"**
- The **Marketing Concept** emerged — put the customer at the centre of everything
- **Market research** became critical — understanding customers before making products
- **Integrated marketing** — all business functions aligned to serve the customer
- Long-term customer satisfaction and **profitability** as goals
- Birth of **market segmentation, targeting, and positioning**

**Management Focus:** Customer needs, market research, product development, customer satisfaction

**The Marketing Concept has 3 pillars:**
1. **Customer Orientation** — understand and satisfy customer needs
2. **Integrated Marketing** — all departments work together for the customer
3. **Profitability** — satisfy customers profitably (not at any cost)

**Example:** Companies like P&G, Unilever — extensive consumer research before product development

**Limitation:** Focused primarily on individual customer satisfaction — broader social and environmental consequences were ignored.

---

## Era 5: 🌍 The Societal/Holistic Marketing Era (1990s–Present)

**Core Belief:**
> *"Satisfy customer needs while also considering the long-term welfare of society and the environment."*

**Key Characteristics:**
- Evolved from the Marketing Concept — adds **social responsibility** and **sustainability**
- Companies must balance: **customer wants + company profits + societal well-being**
- Rise of **Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)**
- **Green marketing** — environmentally friendly products and practices
- **Relationship marketing** — building lasting customer relationships
- **Digital marketing** — internet, social media, data analytics
- **Co-creation** — customers participate in product development

**Management Focus:** Customer value, social responsibility, sustainability, long-term relationships, digital channels

**The Societal Marketing Concept balances THREE considerations:**
```
        SOCIETY
     (Human Welfare)
           △
           │
           │
CONSUMERS ─┤─ COMPANY
(Wants)    │  (Profits)
```

**Sub-concepts within the Societal Era:**

| Concept | Description |
|---|---|
| **Relationship Marketing** | Building long-term mutually beneficial relationships with customers, suppliers, distributors |
| **Integrated Marketing** | All marketing communications deliver a consistent message |
| **Internal Marketing** | Marketing the company to its own employees — engaged employees create better customer experiences |
| **Performance Marketing** | Measuring financial returns AND social/ethical/environmental impact of marketing activities |
| **Digital/Online Marketing** | Using internet, social media, SEO, email, content marketing |

---

## 🔷 Summary of Marketing Eras

| Era | Period | Core Focus | Key Question | Approach |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Production** | Pre-1920s | Manufacturing efficiency | "How do we make more?" | "Make it and they'll buy it" |
| **Product** | 1920s-30s | Product quality | "How do we make it better?" | "Best product wins" |
| **Sales** | 1930s-50s | Aggressive selling | "How do we sell it?" | "Sell what we make" |
| **Marketing** | 1950s-90s | Customer needs | "What does the customer want?" | "Make what we can sell" |
| **Societal** | 1990s-Now | Customer + Society + Planet | "How do we create value for all?" | "Long-term sustainable value" |

---

## 🔷 Modern Marketing Concepts

### Relationship Marketing
Building **long-term, mutually beneficial relationships** with customers, suppliers, and partners — not just making one-time sales. The goal is **customer retention and loyalty** over customer lifetime value.

### Digital Marketing
Using **digital channels** — websites, social media, email, SEO, content marketing, influencer marketing, paid digital advertising — to reach and engage customers.

### Green/Sustainable Marketing
Developing and promoting products in ways that are **environmentally responsible** — reducing carbon footprint, using sustainable materials, promoting eco-friendly consumption.

### Social Marketing
Using marketing techniques to **change behaviour for social good** — anti-smoking campaigns, road safety, public health initiatives.

## 🔷 What is the Marketing Mix?

The **Marketing Mix** is the **set of controllable, tactical marketing tools** that a firm uses to produce the response it wants from its target market. It is the combination of decisions a marketer makes about:

- **WHAT** to offer (Product)
- **HOW MUCH** to charge (Price)
- **WHERE** to make it available (Place/Distribution)
- **HOW** to communicate about it (Promotion)

These are the famous **4 Ps of Marketing,** introduced by **E. Jerome McCarthy** in 1960 and popularised by **Philip Kotler.**

> **Philip Kotler's Definition:**
> *"Marketing mix is the set of marketing tools that the firm uses to pursue its marketing objectives in the target market."*

> **Neil Borden** first coined the term "Marketing Mix" in 1964.

---

## 🔷 The 4 Ps of Marketing Mix

```
              MARKETING MIX
                    │
        ┌───────────┼───────────┐
        ▼           ▼           ▼           ▼
    PRODUCT       PRICE       PLACE     PROMOTION
  (What you     (What you   (Where it  (How you tell
    offer)       charge)    is sold)    people about it)
```

---

## 🅰️ P1 — PRODUCT

**Meaning:** A product is **anything offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use, or consumption** that might satisfy a want or need. It includes physical goods, services, experiences, persons, places, organisations, and ideas.

**Levels of a Product (Philip Kotler's 5 Levels):**

```
Level 5: POTENTIAL PRODUCT
(All possible future augmentations)
                 ▲
Level 4: AUGMENTED PRODUCT
(After-sales service, warranty, delivery)
                 ▲
Level 3: EXPECTED PRODUCT
(Attributes customers expect — clean hotel room, working phone)
                 ▲
Level 2: ACTUAL / BASIC PRODUCT
(Features, brand name, quality, design, packaging)
                 ▲
Level 1: CORE PRODUCT / CORE BENEFIT
(The fundamental benefit the customer is really buying)
Example: Customer buys a drill → Core benefit is HOLES, not the drill
```

**Product Mix Decisions:**

| Decision | Description |
|---|---|
| **Product Line** | A group of related products marketed by the same firm |
| **Product Width** | Number of different product lines a company offers |
| **Product Depth** | Number of product variants within each product line |
| **Product Length** | Total number of items in all product lines |
| **Product Consistency** | How closely related the different product lines are |

---

## 🅱️ P2 — PRICE

**Meaning:** Price is the **amount of money charged for a product or service** — or the sum of all values that customers exchange for the benefit of having or using the product.

Price is the **only element of the Marketing Mix that generates revenue** — all others generate costs.

**Factors Affecting Price:**

| Internal Factors | External Factors |
|---|---|
| Cost of production | Demand and consumer perception |
| Marketing objectives | Competition |
| Organisational considerations | Economic conditions |
| Product positioning | Government regulations |
| Company image | Distribution channel expectations |

**Major Pricing Strategies:**

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost-Plus Pricing** | Add a standard markup to cost | Manufacturing cost ₹100 + 30% = ₹130 |
| **Competitive Pricing** | Price based on what competitors charge | Petrol stations match each other's prices |
| **Value-Based Pricing** | Price based on perceived customer value | Luxury brands charge premium for perceived quality |
| **Penetration Pricing** | Low initial price to gain market share quickly | New streaming service at ₹99/month |
| **Skimming Pricing** | High initial price, then lower over time | New iPhones launch at premium prices |
| **Psychological Pricing** | Price to influence perception | ₹999 feels much cheaper than ₹1000 |

---

## 🅲 P3 — PLACE (Distribution)

**Meaning:** Place refers to **all activities that make the product available to target customers** — the distribution channels, locations, logistics, and supply chain management that ensure the right product reaches the right customer at the right time and place.

**Distribution Channel Levels:**

```
LEVEL 0 (Direct): Producer → Consumer
LEVEL 1:          Producer → Retailer → Consumer
LEVEL 2:          Producer → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer
LEVEL 3:          Producer → Agent → Wholesaler → Retailer → Consumer
```

**Types of Distribution:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Intensive Distribution** | Available in as many outlets as possible | Coca-Cola — available everywhere |
| **Selective Distribution** | Available in selected outlets | Electronics brands in select stores |
| **Exclusive Distribution** | Available in very few/single outlets | Luxury cars — limited dealers |

---

## 🅳 P4 — PROMOTION

**Meaning:** Promotion refers to **all communication activities** that inform, persuade, and remind target customers about the product — creating awareness, interest, desire, and action.

**Elements of the Promotion Mix (5 Elements):**

| Element | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Advertising** | Paid, non-personal mass communication | TV ads, newspaper ads, digital ads |
| **Sales Promotion** | Short-term incentives to stimulate purchase | Discounts, coupons, buy-1-get-1 offers |
| **Personal Selling** | Direct, face-to-face selling by salespeople | Insurance agents, car salespeople |
| **Public Relations (PR)** | Building positive image through unpaid media | Press releases, events, sponsorships |
| **Direct Marketing** | Direct communication with targeted individuals | Email marketing, telemarketing, direct mail |

---

## 🔷 Extended Marketing Mix: The 7 Ps (For Services)

Because services are **intangible, inseparable, variable, and perishable** (IIVP), the traditional 4Ps are expanded with **3 additional Ps** for service marketing:

| Additional P | Description |
|---|---|
| **People** | All human actors in service delivery — employees and customers. Service quality depends heavily on the people delivering it. |
| **Process** | The procedures, mechanisms, and flow of activities by which a service is delivered. Efficient, customer-friendly processes improve satisfaction. |
| **Physical Evidence** | The tangible cues in the service environment that help customers evaluate intangible services — store design, uniforms, brochures, equipment. |

---

## 🔷 Interrelationship of the Marketing Mix

The 4Ps must be **integrated and mutually consistent:**

- A **premium product** (Product) demands a **premium price** (Price), **selective distribution** in upscale stores (Place), and **image-building advertising** (Promotion)
- A **budget product** needs **low prices, wide distribution, and mass promotion**
- Any inconsistency weakens the overall marketing strategy

---

## 🔷 Evolution from 4Ps to 4Cs

Critics argued the 4Ps were **producer-centric.** Robert Lauterborn proposed the **4Cs** — a customer-centric equivalent:

| 4Ps (Seller's View) | 4Cs (Customer's View) |
|---|---|
| **Product** | **Customer Solution** — Does it solve the customer's problem? |
| **Price** | **Customer Cost** — What is the total cost to the customer? |
| **Place** | **Convenience** — How convenient is it to buy? |
| **Promotion** | **Communication** — Two-way dialogue, not just one-way advertising |

# 📌 CHAPTER 4: MARKETING ENVIRONMENT — CONCEPT, IMPORTANCE AND COMPONENTS

---

## 🔷 What is Marketing Environment?

The **Marketing Environment** consists of all the **forces and actors outside (and inside) the marketing function** that affect marketing management's ability to build and maintain successful relationships with target customers.

No business operates in a vacuum. The marketing environment presents both **opportunities** (favourable factors to exploit) and **threats** (unfavourable factors to counter or avoid).

> **Philip Kotler:**
> *"A company's marketing environment consists of the actors and forces outside marketing that affect marketing management's ability to build and maintain successful relationships with target customers."*

---

## 🔷 Importance of Studying Marketing Environment

**1. Identifies Opportunities**
Environmental scanning reveals **new market opportunities** — new customer segments, unmet needs, emerging trends — that the company can exploit.

**2. Anticipates Threats**
Early awareness of **threats** (new competitors, changing regulations, economic downturns) gives companies time to prepare and respond proactively.

**3. Basis for Strategic Planning**
Understanding the environment provides the **foundation for all marketing planning** — you cannot plan effectively without knowing the environment you are operating in.

**4. Helps in Adaptation**
Companies that understand their environment can **adapt their marketing mix** — products, prices, distribution, promotion — to match changing conditions.

**5. Ensures Relevance**
Staying attuned to environmental changes ensures the company's offerings remain **relevant to customers** whose needs evolve with the environment.

**6. Guides Resource Allocation**
Environmental analysis helps managers **prioritise where to invest** — which markets to enter, which products to develop, which customers to target.

---

The marketing environment is divided into two major levels:

# 🅰️ MICRO ENVIRONMENT (Near Environment / Task Environment)

The **micro environment** consists of the forces **close to the company** that directly affect its ability to serve its customers. These are factors the company has **some degree of influence over.**

---

### 1. 🏢 The Company Itself (Internal Environment)
The marketing department must work in harmony with other departments:
- **Top management** sets overall strategy and approves marketing budgets
- **Finance** provides funds for marketing activities
- **R&D** develops products that marketing must sell
- **Production** must deliver what marketing promises
- **HR** hires and trains people marketing needs

All departments must be **coordinated and aligned** to deliver customer value.

---

### 2. 🏪 Suppliers
Suppliers provide the **raw materials, components, and resources** that the company needs to produce its goods and services.

**Impact on Marketing:**
- Supplier shortages or price increases → **increased costs → higher prices → reduced competitiveness**
- Poor quality supplies → **poor quality products → customer dissatisfaction**
- Unreliable suppliers → **delays in delivery → unhappy customers**

Modern companies build **supplier relationship management (SRM)** systems to ensure reliable, quality supply.

---

### 3. 🔗 Marketing Intermediaries (Channel Members)
Intermediaries help the company **reach, promote, sell, and distribute** its products to end buyers.

| Type | Role | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Resellers** | Buy and resell products | Wholesalers, retailers, distributors |
| **Physical Distribution Firms** | Store and move goods | Warehouses, logistics companies, courier firms |
| **Marketing Services Agencies** | Assist in promotion | Advertising agencies, market research firms, PR firms |
| **Financial Intermediaries** | Finance transactions | Banks, insurance companies, credit companies |

---

### 4. 👥 Customers (Markets)

The most critical micro environmental force — **customers are the entire purpose of marketing.**

| Market Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Consumer Markets** | Individuals/households buying for personal consumption |
| **Business Markets** | Companies buying for production purposes |
| **Reseller Markets** | Intermediaries buying to resell |
| **Government Markets** | Government agencies buying for public services |
| **International Markets** | Buyers in other countries |

---

### 5. 🥊 Competitors

Companies must **monitor competitors** closely — understanding their strategies, strengths, weaknesses, and moves — to design marketing strategies that give competitive advantages.

**Types of Competition:**
- **Brand Competition:** Direct competitors selling similar products (Pepsi vs Coca-Cola)
- **Product Form Competition:** Different types of the same product (regular cola vs diet cola)
- **Generic Competition:** Products that satisfy the same need differently (cola vs juice vs water)
- **Total Budget Competition:** All products competing for the same consumer rupees

---

### 6. 👥 Publics

A **public** is any group that has an actual or potential interest in or impact on the company's ability to achieve its objectives.

| Type of Public | Description |
|---|---|
| **Financial Publics** | Banks, investors, shareholders who affect funding |
| **Media Publics** | Newspapers, TV, online media — can build or destroy reputation |
| **Government Publics** | Government agencies — regulations, policies |
| **Citizen-Action Publics** | Consumer groups, environmental groups |
| **Internal Publics** | Employees, managers, the board |
| **Local Publics** | Neighbours and local community |
| **General Public** | Society at large — public perception matters |

---

# 🅱️ MACRO ENVIRONMENT (Far Environment / PESTLE)

The **macro environment** consists of the **large societal forces** that affect all players in the micro environment. These are **largely uncontrollable** by the individual firm — but must be monitored and adapted to.

The major macro environment forces are captured in the **PESTLE framework:**

---

### 1. 🏛️ Political/Legal Environment

Government policies, laws, and regulations that affect business:

- **Trade policies** — import/export regulations, tariffs
- **Tax laws** — GST, corporate tax, excise duties
- **Labour laws** — minimum wage, working conditions
- **Consumer protection laws** — standards, warranties, advertising regulations
- **Competition laws** — anti-monopoly regulations
- **Environmental regulations** — pollution control, packaging standards

**Marketing Impact:**
- Regulations on advertising content (e.g., no misleading claims)
- Import duties affect price of imported raw materials
- Government subsidies/incentives create opportunities

---

### 2. 📊 Economic Environment

The **economic conditions** that affect consumer purchasing power and spending patterns:

- **Economic growth rate** — high GDP growth → more consumer spending → market opportunity
- **Interest rates** — affect cost of borrowing and consumer credit

# 📌 CHAPTER 5: CONSUMER BEHAVIOUR — NATURE, IMPORTANCE, PROCESS AND FACTORS INFLUENCING

---

## 🔷 What is Consumer Behaviour?

**Consumer Behaviour** is the study of **how individuals, groups, and organisations select, buy, use, and dispose of goods, services, ideas, or experiences** to satisfy their needs and wants — and the **factors that influence these processes.**

Understanding consumer behaviour is critical because it answers the fundamental marketing question:

> **"Why do people buy what they buy?"**

> **Leon G. Schiffman and Leslie Lazar Kanuk:**
> *"Consumer behaviour is the behaviour that consumers display in searching for, purchasing, using, evaluating and disposing of products and services that they expect will satisfy their needs."*

> **Engel, Blackwell and Miniard:**
> *"Consumer behaviour is the actions and decision processes of people who purchase goods and services for personal consumption."*

---

## 🔷 Nature of Consumer Behaviour

### 1. 🔄 Dynamic
Consumer behaviour is **constantly changing** — preferences, tastes, attitudes, and decision processes evolve with time, culture, experience, and exposure to information.

### 2. 🧠 Complex
Consumer decisions involve **complex psychological, social, and situational factors** — often difficult to predict or fully understand.

### 3. 👤 Individual and Group
Consumer behaviour occurs at both the **individual level** (personal decision-making) and the **group level** (family decisions, peer influence, cultural norms).

### 4. 🌐 Varies Across Cultures
Consumer behaviour differs significantly **across countries, regions, religions, and cultures** — what sells in one market may fail in another.

### 5. 🔬 Interdisciplinary
Consumer behaviour draws from **psychology** (individual motivation, perception), **sociology** (group influence), **anthropology** (culture), **economics** (decision-making under constraints), and **neuroscience** (how the brain processes marketing stimuli).

### 6. 🌱 Process-Oriented
Consumer behaviour is not just about the moment of purchase — it is a **process** that begins with problem recognition and continues after purchase with post-purchase evaluation.

---

## 🔷 Importance of Studying Consumer Behaviour

### 🔹 For Marketers / Companies:

**1. Better Product Design**
Understanding what consumers need and value enables companies to design **products that truly satisfy those needs** — reducing product failure rates.

**2. Effective Market Segmentation**
Consumer behaviour insights reveal **meaningful differences between customer groups** — enabling better segmentation, targeting, and positioning.

**3. Optimal Pricing**
Understanding how consumers perceive price and value helps in setting **prices that customers find acceptable** while maintaining profitability.

**4. Efficient Distribution**
Knowing where and when consumers shop helps design **distribution strategies** that make products available at the right place and time.

**5. Effective Communication**
Understanding consumer psychology, attitudes, and motivations helps create **marketing messages that resonate** — the right message, in the right tone, through the right medium.

**6. Predicting Market Trends**
Consumer behaviour research identifies **emerging trends** — giving forward-thinking companies competitive advantage.

**7. Building Customer Loyalty**
Understanding what creates satisfaction (and dissatisfaction) after purchase helps design **post-purchase strategies** that build loyalty and reduce churn.

### 🔹 For Society:

**1. Consumer Education**
Consumer behaviour insights help design **effective consumer education campaigns** — teaching people to make better purchasing decisions.

**2. Public Policy**
Governments use consumer behaviour insights to design **effective public health and safety campaigns** and consumer protection regulations.

---

## 🔷 The Consumer Decision-Making Process (6 Stages)

```
STAGE 1: PROBLEM / NEED RECOGNITION
Consumer realises there is a gap between their
current state and their desired state
(e.g., phone is broken → need a new phone)
                ↓
STAGE 2: INFORMATION SEARCH
Consumer actively seeks information about
solutions to their problem
• Internal search: Memory and past experience
• External search: Friends, family, internet,
  advertising, salespeople, product reviews
                ↓
STAGE 3: EVALUATION OF ALTERNATIVES
Consumer evaluates options based on criteria
that matter to them (price, quality, brand,
features, reviews)
• Creates an "evoked set" — short list of
  acceptable alternatives
                ↓
STAGE 4: PURCHASE DECISION
Consumer selects one alternative and makes
the purchase (but may be influenced at the
last moment by situational factors or
unexpected barriers)
                ↓
STAGE 5: PURCHASE BEHAVIOUR
The actual act of buying — choosing brand,
retailer, quantity, payment method, timing
                ↓
STAGE 6: POST-PURCHASE EVALUATION
Consumer compares actual product performance
with expectations:
• If Performance ≥ Expectations → Satisfied
• If Performance < Expectations → Dissatisfied
→ Cognitive Dissonance (buyer's remorse)?
→ Word-of-mouth, brand loyalty, or switching?
```

---

### 🔷 Cognitive Dissonance (Post-Purchase Doubt)

**Cognitive dissonance** is the **psychological discomfort** a buyer feels after making a significant purchase — wondering if they made the right choice.

**Why it occurs:**
- The unchosen alternatives had attractive features
- The purchase was expensive or irreversible
- The consumer had low confidence in their choice

**Marketing Response:**
- After-sales calls and follow-ups
- Reassuring advertising ("You made the right choice")
- Excellent after-sales service and warranty support
- Clear usage instructions and support resources

---

## 🔷 Types of Consumer Buying Behaviour

Based on **degree of involvement** and **degree of brand difference:**

| Type | Involvement | Brand Difference | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Complex Buying** | High | High | Extensive research; significant decision | Buying a car, laptop, house |
| **Dissonance-Reducing** | High | Low | High involvement but brands are similar; buyer may experience post-purchase doubt | Buying flooring, furniture |
| **Habitual Buying** | Low | Low | Routine purchase; little thought | Buying salt, bread, petrol |
| **Variety-Seeking** | Low | High | Low involvement but frequently switches brands out of variety/curiosity | Buying biscuits, soft drinks |

## 🔷 Factors Influencing Consumer Behaviour

Consumer behaviour is influenced by a complex web of **cultural, social, personal, and psychological factors:**

---

### 🅰️ CULTURAL FACTORS (Most Fundamental)

**1. Culture**
The broadest and most fundamental influence. Culture is the **set of basic values, perceptions, wants, and behaviours** that a person learns from family and society.

- Culture defines what is desirable, acceptable, taboo, or fashionable
- Marketers must understand the **cultural context** of their target market
- **Cross-cultural marketing** challenges — what works in one culture may offend in another

> **Example:** McDonald's offers a McAloo Tikki burger in India (culturally adapted product) and does not serve beef — adapting to local cultural values.

**2. Subculture**
Groups of people within a larger culture who share **distinct values, beliefs, or behaviours:**
- **Nationality** — e.g., South Indian vs North Indian food preferences
- **Religion** — Halal, Kosher, vegetarian product preferences
- **Region** — Northeast Indian preferences differ from West Indian
- **Age group** — Gen Z, Millennials, Baby Boomers

**3. Social Class**
Relatively permanent and ordered **divisions in society** whose members share similar values, interests, and behaviours. In India, commonly classified as:
- Upper class, Upper-middle, Middle, Lower-middle, Lower

Social class influences: product choices, shopping locations, media consumption, price sensitivity, brand preferences.

---

### 🅱️ SOCIAL FACTORS

**1. Reference Groups**
Groups that **serve as comparison points** for a person's attitudes and behaviour:

| Type | Description | Marketing Impact |
|---|---|---|
| **Membership Groups** | Groups the person belongs to (friends, family, colleagues) | Word-of-mouth recommendations |
| **Aspirational Groups** | Groups the person wants to belong to (celebrities, successful people) | Celebrity endorsements |
| **Dissociative Groups** | Groups the person wants to distance from | Positioning away from certain group associations |

**2. Family**
The **most important reference group** for most consumer decisions:

| Stage | Consumer Behaviour Implication |
|---|---|
| **Husband-Wife Influence** | Who decides what to buy in a household? |
| **Children's Influence** | Children influence family purchases (toys, cereals, holidays) |
| **Family Life Cycle** | Young single → Young couple → Family with young children → Empty nesters → Retired — each stage has different needs |

**3. Roles and Status**
People occupy **roles** (parent, professional, citizen) and have associated **status.** Products are often chosen to communicate role and status.

> **Example:** A senior manager might buy a premium car to signal professional status.

---

### 🅲 PERSONAL FACTORS

**1. Age and Life Cycle Stage**
Consumer tastes and needs **change with age:**
- Infants and children → toys, baby food, diapers
- Teenagers → fashion, music, electronics, social experiences
- Young adults → career wear, first home, travel
- Middle age → investment products, health products, children's education
- Seniors → healthcare, leisure, comfort products

**2. Occupation and Economic Situation**
A person's job influences **what they buy:**
- Blue-collar workers vs white-collar professionals have different product, brand, and media preferences
- Income level determines what is affordable
- Savings vs spending attitudes affect purchasing

**3. Lifestyle**
A person's **pattern of living** — activities, interests, and opinions (AIO) — determines consumption patterns.

**Lifestyle profiles:**
- Health-conscious → fitness club memberships, organic food, wearable health trackers
- Tech enthusiast → early adopter of gadgets, premium tech subscriptions
- Eco-conscious → sustainable brands, electric vehicles, organic products

**4. Personality and Self-Concept**
Each person has a unique **personality** — self-confidence, sociability, autonomy, adaptability — that influences brand choices.

**Self-concept:** People buy brands that match their **actual self-image** (who they are) or their **ideal self-image** (who they want to be).

> **Example:** Someone who sees themselves as adventurous will prefer brands that project adventure (Red Bull, Royal Enfield).

---

### 🅳 PSYCHOLOGICAL FACTORS

**1. Motivation**
The **driving force** behind consumer behaviour — an unsatisfied need creates tension that motivates behaviour to satisfy it.

**Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs** (applied to marketing):

```
           ▲ SELF-ACTUALISATION
           │ (Fulfilment, luxury experiences, self-improvement)
           ▲ ESTEEM NEEDS
           │ (Status symbols, premium brands, recognition products)
           ▲ SOCIAL NEEDS
           │ (Social media platforms, group memberships, greeting cards)
           ▲ SAFETY NEEDS
           │ (Insurance, home security, helmets, health checkups)
           ▲ PHYSIOLOGICAL NEEDS
             (Food, water, clothing, shelter, sleep — basic products)
```

**2. Perception**
The process by which a person **selects, organises, and interprets information** to create a meaningful picture of the world.

Key concepts:
- **Selective Attention** — People pay attention to only some of the thousands of marketing messages they encounter daily (those relevant to them or unusual)
- **Selective Distortion** — People interpret information in a way that fits their existing beliefs
- **Selective Retention** — People remember information that supports their attitudes and forget contradictory information

**Marketing Implication:** Great products fail because of poor perception. Average products succeed because of excellent perception management.

**3. Learning**
**Changes in behaviour** that result from experience. When consumers buy and use products, they learn from that experience — which influences future purchases.

- **Classical conditioning** — Brand associations (Fevicol → strength)
- **Operant conditioning** — Reward programmes (loyalty points for purchases)
- **Cognitive learning** — Information about benefits influences rational decisions

**4. Beliefs and Attitudes**
- **Belief** — A descriptive thought a person holds about something (e.g., "Brand X laptops are reliable")
- **Attitude** — A person's relatively consistent evaluations, feelings, and tendencies toward an object (e.g., "I love Apple products")

**Marketing Challenge:** It is very hard and expensive to change strongly held attitudes. It is easier to design products that match existing attitudes.

# 📌 CHAPTER 6: MARKET SEGMENTATION — CONCEPT, IMPORTANCE AND BASES

---

## 🔷 What is Market Segmentation?

**Market Segmentation** is the process of **dividing a large, heterogeneous market into smaller, homogeneous sub-groups (segments)** of consumers who share similar characteristics, needs, wants, and responses to marketing.

The fundamental insight behind segmentation is that **not all customers are alike.** Different customers have different needs, different budgets, different preferences, and different responses to marketing. One single marketing approach cannot effectively serve everyone.

> **Philip Kotler:**
> *"Market segmentation is the act of dividing a market into distinct groups of buyers who have different needs, characteristics, or behaviour, and who might require separate products or marketing programmes."*

> **William Stanton:**
> *"Market segmentation consists of taking the total heterogeneous market for a product and dividing it into several sub-markets or segments, each of which tends to be homogeneous in all significant aspects."*

---

## 🔷 The STP Framework (Segmentation → Targeting → Positioning)

Market segmentation is the **first step** in a three-step strategic process:

```
SEGMENTATION          TARGETING               POSITIONING
Divide the market → Select the best     → Position the offering
into segments        segment(s) to serve   distinctly in the
                                           chosen segment's mind
```

*(Targeting and Positioning are covered in Chapter 7)*

---

## 🔷 Importance / Benefits of Market Segmentation

**1. Better Understanding of Customers**
Segmentation forces companies to **deeply analyse different customer groups** — their needs, motivations, purchase behaviour — leading to better customer insight.

**2. More Effective Marketing**
Tailored marketing messages and offerings are **far more effective** than generic approaches. Targeted communication resonates better and drives higher response rates.

**3. Efficient Use of Marketing Resources**
Instead of wasting resources on everyone, segmentation allows companies to **concentrate resources on the most profitable and accessible segments.**

**4. Competitive Advantage**
By serving specific segments exceptionally well, companies can **build strong positions** that are difficult for competitors to attack.

**5. Product Differentiation**
Segmentation reveals opportunities to **differentiate products** for different customer groups — multiple variants, flavours, price points, or features.

**6. Identifies Market Opportunities**
Segmentation analysis often reveals **underserved or unserved segments** — representing new market opportunities.

**7. Improves Customer Satisfaction**
When products and marketing are tailored to specific needs, **customer satisfaction is higher** — leading to loyalty and repeat purchase.

**8. Facilitates Pricing Strategy**
Different segments have different price sensitivities — segmentation enables **differential pricing** (premium pricing for some, value pricing for others).

**9. Helps in Channel Selection**
Different segments may be reached through **different distribution channels** — segmentation guides channel strategy.

---

## 🔷 Requirements for Effective Market Segmentation

For a segment to be useful, it must satisfy **5 criteria (MASDA):**

| Criterion | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Measurable** | The segment's size, purchasing power, and characteristics must be measurable. |
| **Accessible** | The segment must be effectively reached and served through available distribution and communication channels. |
| **Substantial** | The segment must be large and profitable enough to be worth targeting. |
| **Differentiable** | Segments must be conceptually distinguishable and respond differently to different marketing mix elements. |
| **Actionable** | Effective programmes can be formulated to attract and serve the segments. |

---

## 🔷 Bases / Variables for Market Segmentation

### 🅰️ GEOGRAPHIC SEGMENTATION

**Dividing the market based on location and geography:**

| Variable | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Region** | North India, South India, Northeast India, Urban vs Rural |
| **Country/Nation** | India, USA, China, Bangladesh |
| **City Size** | Metro cities (>50 lakh), Large cities, Small towns, Villages |
| **Density** | Urban, Suburban, Rural |
| **Climate** | Hot/tropical, Cold/temperate, Desert, Coastal |

**Marketing Application:**
- A raincoat company targets **high-rainfall regions** (Northeast India, Kerala)
- A winter clothing brand focuses on **cold-climate regions** (Kashmir, North India)
- A fast food chain may offer **different menus** in North India vs South India

**Advantages:** Easy to identify, measure, and reach geographically defined segments

---

### 🅱️ DEMOGRAPHIC SEGMENTATION

**Most widely used basis — divides market by measurable population characteristics:**

| Variable | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Age** | Children (0-12), Teens (13-18), Young Adults (18-35), Middle-Aged (35-55), Seniors (55+) |
| **Gender** | Male, Female, Non-binary |
| **Income** | Low-income (< ₹2L/year), Middle-income (₹2-10L), Upper-middle (₹10-25L), High-income (> ₹25L) |
| **Education** | School dropout, School graduate, Graduate, Post-graduate, Professional |
| **Occupation** | Student, Professional, Blue-collar, Farmer, Business owner, Retired |
| **Family Size** | Single, Couple (no children), Small family (1-2 children), Large family (3+ children) |
| **Family Life Cycle** | Bachelor, Newly married, Full nest, Empty nester, Retired |
| **Religion** | Hindu, Muslim, Christian, Sikh, Jain, Buddhist |
| **Social Class** | Lower, Middle, Upper-middle, Upper |
| **Generation** | Baby Boomers, Gen X, Millennials, Gen Z |

**Marketing Application:**
- Johnson's Baby products → **infants and young children segment**
- Titan Raga watches → **women segment**
- Maruti Alto → **low-to-middle income segment**
- BMW → **high-income, status-conscious segment**

**Advantages:** Easy to measure; correlates strongly with needs and purchasing power

---

### 🅲 PSYCHOGRAPHIC SEGMENTATION

**Divides consumers based on psychological characteristics — lifestyle, values, personality, and attitudes:**

| Variable | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Lifestyle** | Health-conscious, Tech-savvy, Eco-friendly, Luxury-oriented, Value-seeker, Adventure-seeker |
| **Personality** | Ambitious, Sociable, Conservative, Rebellious, Creative, Traditional |
| **Values and Beliefs** | Environmental consciousness, Religious conservatism, Progressive thinking |
| **Interests** | Sports, Music, Travel, Fashion, Cooking, Gaming, Reading |
| **Activities** | Gym-goers, Online shoppers, Social media users, Outdoor enthusiasts |
| **Opinions** | Attitudes toward health, environment, technology, politics |

**VALS Framework (Values and Lifestyles System):**

Developed by SRI International, VALS classifies consumers into types based on resources (income, education) and primary motivation (ideals, achievement, self-expression):

| VALS Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Innovators** | Successful, sophisticated, high resources, receptive to new ideas |
| **Thinkers** | Mature, reflective, value knowledge, order, and responsibility |
| **Achievers** | Goal-oriented, committed to career and family, prefer premium products |
| **Experiencers** | Young, enthusiastic, seek variety and excitement |
| **Believers** | Conservative, conventional, traditional values |
| **Strivers** | Seek approval, trendy, low resources |
| **Makers** | Practical, self-sufficient, prefer functional products |
| **Survivors** | Focused on daily needs, lowest resources, brand loyal |

**Advantages:** Rich, deep understanding of customers; explains the "why" behind purchases

**Limitations:** Hard to measure; segments are not always easy to reach

---

### 🅳 BEHAVIOURAL SEGMENTATION

**Divides consumers based on actual behaviour — knowledge, attitudes, use, or response to a product:**

| Variable | Sub-categories |
|---|---|
| **Occasion** | Regular occasion, Special occasion, Festival occasion (Diwali buying, Birthday gifts) |
| **Benefits Sought** | Quality seekers, Price seekers, Service seekers, Convenience seekers, Status seekers |
| **User Status** | Non-users, Ex-users, Potential users, First-time users, Regular users |
| **Usage Rate** | Light users, Medium users, Heavy users |
| **Loyalty Status** | No loyalty, Shifting loyalty, Split loyalty, Hard-core loyal |
| **Readiness Stage** | Unaware, Aware, Informed, Interested, Desirous, Intending to buy |
| **Attitude Toward Product** | Enthusiastic, Positive, Indifferent, Negative, Hostile |

**The 80-20 Rule (Pareto Principle):**
In most markets, **80% of revenue comes from 20% of customers (heavy users).** Marketers often focus disproportionate attention on this **heavy user segment.**

**Marketing Application:**
- **Occasion segmentation:** Archies greeting cards → birthday, anniversary, Valentine's Day
- **Benefit segmentation:** Colgate → whitening (cosmetic benefit), Cavity protection (health benefit), Sensitivity (medical benefit)
- **Loyalty segmentation:** Airlines have Frequent Flyer programmes specifically for **loyal heavy users**

---

### 🔷 Multi-Variable Segmentation

In practice, companies rarely use just one segmentation variable — they combine **multiple variables** to create **richer, more precise segments:**

> **Example:** A premium fitness app targets:
> - **Geography:** Urban metro cities (Geographic)
> - **Demographics:** Age 25-40, upper-middle income, college-educated (Demographic)
> - **Psychographics:** Health-conscious, achievement-oriented, busy professional (Psychographic)
> - **Behaviour:** Regular gym users, early technology adopters (Behavioural)

## 🔷 Levels of Market Segmentation

| Level | Description | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Mass Marketing** | One product for everyone | No segmentation (e.g., basic commodities) |
| **Segment Marketing** | Different offerings for different large segments | Broad segmentation (e.g., economy vs premium cars) |
| **Niche Marketing** | Highly targeted offering for a small, specialised segment | Deep segmentation (e.g., vegan leather shoes) |
| **Micro/Local Marketing** | Tailored for very small local groups | Geographic + demographic (e.g., local dialect advertising) |
| **Individual/Personalised Marketing** | Customised for each individual customer | Big data driven (e.g., Amazon recommendations) |

# 📌 CHAPTER 7: TARGET MARKET SELECTION AND POSITIONING CONCEPT

---

## 🔷 Target Market Selection (TARGETING)

After segmenting the market, the company must evaluate each segment and decide **which ones to enter** — this is called **targeting.**

> **Philip Kotler:**
> *"A target market is a set of buyers sharing common needs or characteristics that the company decides to serve."*

---

## 🔷 Evaluating Market Segments

Before selecting target segments, evaluate each on **3 dimensions:**

**1. Segment Size and Growth**
- Is the segment currently large enough to be profitable?
- Is it growing, stable, or declining?
- What will it look like in 3-5 years?

**2. Segment Structural Attractiveness**
Using Porter's Five Forces:
- How intense is existing competition?
- Are there powerful buyers who could squeeze margins?
- Are there powerful suppliers?
- Is there a threat of substitutes?
- How easy is it for new entrants to join?

**3. Company Objectives and Resources**
- Does this segment fit the company's long-term objectives?
- Does the company have the capabilities (technology, skills, resources) to serve it well?
- Even if attractive, can the company realistically compete here?

---

## 🔷 Target Market Selection Strategies

### Strategy 1: 🌊 Undifferentiated (Mass) Marketing

**Approach:** Ignore segment differences; design a **single offer for the whole market.**

**Logic:** What's common across all customers, not what's different.

**When to use:** Homogeneous market; commodity products; the company wants scale economies

**Example:** Basic table salt — Tata Salt markets to everyone essentially the same way

**Advantages:** Maximum economies of scale; simplest to manage; low cost

**Disadvantages:** Cannot satisfy different segments well; increasingly ineffective in diverse markets

---

### Strategy 2: 🎯 Differentiated Marketing

**Approach:** Target **several different segments** with **different marketing mix offerings** for each.

**When to use:** Different segments have meaningfully different needs; company has resources to serve multiple segments

**Example:** HUL sells Dove (premium, moisturising) AND Lifebuoy (mass market, health) AND Lux (beauty) — different soaps for different segments

**Advantages:** Higher total sales; stronger position in each segment; diversified risk

**Disadvantages:** Higher costs (multiple products, multiple campaigns, multiple distributions); potential brand confusion

---

### Strategy 3: 🔭 Concentrated (Niche) Marketing

**Approach:** Target **one specific segment** and serve it very well.

**When to use:** Limited resources; highly specialised expertise; strong competition in mass market

**Example:** Rolls-Royce serves only the ultra-luxury car segment; Ferrari targets only high-performance sports car enthusiasts

**Advantages:** Strong market position in the chosen niche; deep customer knowledge; efficient use of limited resources

**Disadvantages:** All eggs in one basket — if the segment shrinks or is attacked, the company is vulnerable

---

### Strategy 4: 🤝 Micromarketing

**Approach:** Tailoring marketing to **individual people or very small local groups.**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Local Marketing** | Tailoring to local groups (cities, neighbourhoods) | Regional language advertising; local food variants |
| **Individual Marketing** | Tailoring to individual customers | Amazon personalised recommendations; customised jerseys |

---

## 🔷 POSITIONING CONCEPT

---

## 🔷 What is Positioning?

**Positioning** is the process of **designing the company's offering and image so that it occupies a distinctive and valued place in the minds of the target customers** — relative to competing products.

The goal of positioning is to **occupy a clear, distinctive, desirable position** in the consumer's mind.

> **Al Ries and Jack Trout (who coined the term):**
> *"Positioning is not what you do to a product. It is what you do to the mind of the prospect. That is, you position the product in the mind of the prospect."*

> **Philip Kotler:**
> *"Positioning is the act of designing the company's offering and image to occupy a distinctive place in the target market's mind."*

---

## 🔷 The Concept of Perceptual Mapping

A **perceptual map** is a visual representation of how consumers perceive competing brands on key dimensions (e.g., price vs quality, traditional vs modern).

```
        HIGH QUALITY
             │
Luxury │ Brand A   Brand B
       │
LOW  ──┼────────────────── HIGH
PRICE  │                  PRICE
       │ Brand D   Brand C
Budget │
             │
        LOW QUALITY
```

Positioning strategy aims to find an **uncrowded, valued position** on such a map.

---

## 🔷 Steps in Positioning

### STEP 1: Identify Possible Competitive Advantages
What **differentiation opportunities** exist?

| Basis of Differentiation | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Product Differentiation** | Superior quality, unique features, innovative design |
| **Service Differentiation** | Faster delivery, better after-sales support, easier returns |
| **Personnel Differentiation** | More knowledgeable, friendlier, more helpful staff |
| **Channel Differentiation** | More accessible, more convenient distribution |
| **Image Differentiation** | Stronger brand image, brand story, values |

### STEP 2: Select the Right Competitive Advantages
Not all differentiation matters to customers. Choose advantages that are:
- **Important** to the target customer
- **Distinctive** — not already offered by competitors
- **Superior** — better than alternatives
- **Communicable** — can be explained clearly
- **Pre-emptive** — hard for competitors to quickly copy
- **Affordable** — buyers can afford to pay for the difference
- **Profitable** — the company can make a profit from it

### STEP 3: Select an Overall Positioning Strategy
The **value proposition** — the full mix of benefits on which the brand is positioned:

| Positioning Strategy | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **More for More** | Premium offering at premium price | Rolex watches |
| **More for the Same** | Higher quality at same price as competition | Toyota vs GM in 1990s |
| **Same for Less** | Same quality at lower price | Budget hotels |
| **Less for Much Less** | Lower quality, much lower price | No-frills airlines |
| **More for Less** | Higher quality AND lower price (ideal, hard to sustain) | Amazon (selection + price) |

### STEP 4: Develop a Positioning Statement
A clear, concise statement summarising the brand's position:

**Formula:**
> *"To [target segment], [Brand] is the [frame of reference / category] that [point of difference / key benefit] because [reason to believe]."*

**Example:**
> *"To busy urban professionals, Swiggy is the food delivery service that brings your favourite restaurant meals to your doorstep in 30 minutes because of our extensive restaurant network and optimised delivery system."*

---

## 🔷 Types of Positioning Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Attribute Positioning** | Based on a specific product attribute | Volvo → Safety; Duracell → Long-lasting |
| **Benefit Positioning** | Based on the benefit offered | Colgate → Cavity protection; Fair & Lovely → Brighter skin |
| **Use/Application Positioning** | Based on how/when product is used | Gatorade → Sports performance hydration |
| **User Positioning** | Based on the type of user | Royal Enfield → Rugged adventurers |
| **Competitor Positioning** | Positioning against the competitor | "We're No. 2, we try harder" (Avis vs Hertz) |
| **Quality/Price Positioning** | Best quality or best value | Bata → Value; Nike → Premium performance |
| **Cultural Symbol Positioning** | Links brand to cultural heritage or symbol | Amul → Taste of India |

# 📌 PART 2: PRODUCT MANAGEMENT

---

## 🔷 What is a Product?

A **product** is anything offered to a market to satisfy a need or want — physical goods, services, experiences, ideas, persons, or places.

> **Philip Kotler:** *"A product is anything that can be offered to a market for attention, acquisition, use or consumption that might satisfy a want or need."*

> **William Stanton:** *"A product is a set of tangible and intangible attributes including packaging, colour, price and services which the buyer may accept as offering want satisfaction."*

---

## 🔷 Five Levels of a Product (Kotler)

```
Level 5 → POTENTIAL PRODUCT
          (All future augmentations and improvements)
Level 4 → AUGMENTED PRODUCT
          (Warranty, after-sales service, free delivery, installation)
Level 3 → EXPECTED PRODUCT
          (Attributes customers normally expect — clean room, working AC)
Level 2 → ACTUAL PRODUCT
          (Brand name, features, quality level, design, packaging)
Level 1 → CORE PRODUCT / CORE BENEFIT
          (The fundamental benefit the customer is buying)
          Example: Customer buys a drill → they really want HOLES
```

---

## 🔷 Importance of Product in Marketing

1. **Foundation of Marketing Mix** — Product decisions come first; all other Ps (price, place, promotion) depend on the product
2. **Source of Revenue** — The product is what generates sales and profit
3. **Basis of Competitive Advantage** — A superior product is the strongest competitive weapon
4. **Determines Brand Image** — Product quality and design shape how the brand is perceived
5. **Fulfils Customer Needs** — The primary purpose is satisfying customer needs and creating value
6. **Drives Business Growth** — New products open new markets and revenue streams
7. **Affects All Other Decisions** — Pricing, distribution, and promotion all depend on the nature of the product

---

## 🔷 Classification of Products

### 🅰️ Based on Durability and Tangibility

| Type | Description | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Non-durable Goods** | Tangible; consumed in one or few uses | Soap, food, shampoo, toothpaste |
| **Durable Goods** | Tangible; used over long periods | TV, refrigerator, car, furniture |
| **Services** | Intangible, inseparable, perishable, variable | Banking, education, haircut, transport |

---

### 🅱️ Based on Consumer Buying Behaviour (Consumer Products)

| Type | Buying Effort | Price | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Convenience Products** | Minimal effort; bought frequently | Low | Bread, milk, biscuits, newspapers |
| **Shopping Products** | Considerable comparison before buying | Medium-High | Clothing, electronics, furniture |
| **Specialty Products** | Unique; buyer will make special effort to find | High | Luxury watches, designer wear, sports cars |
| **Unsought Products** | Consumer doesn't normally think to buy | Varies | Life insurance, encyclopaedias, fire extinguishers |

---

### 🅲 Based on Industrial Use (Industrial/Business Products)

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Materials & Parts** | Go directly into manufacturer's product | Raw cotton, steel rods, microchips |
| **Capital Items** | Aid in production; not part of final product | Machines, factory buildings, computers |
| **Supplies & Services** | Operational items; short-lived | Stationery, lubricants, maintenance services |

---

## 🔷 Product Mix (Product Portfolio)

**Product Mix** = The **complete set of all products and product lines** a company offers for sale.

> **Example:** HUL's product mix includes soaps, shampoos, detergents, food products, and beverages — each is a different product line.

### Key Product Mix Dimensions:

| Dimension | Meaning | Example (HUL) |
|---|---|---|
| **Width** | Number of different product lines | Soaps, Detergents, Foods, Beverages = 4 lines |
| **Length** | Total number of items across all lines | Lux + Dove + Lifebuoy + Surf + Rin + Knorr... |
| **Depth** | Number of variants within a product line | Lux: Rose, Sandal, Aqua, Soft Touch, Velvet |
| **Consistency** | How closely related all product lines are | All are FMCG consumer goods = highly consistent |

### Product Mix Decisions (Strategies):

| Decision | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Expansion** | Add new product lines or new items to existing lines |
| **Contraction** | Drop weak product lines or items |
| **Alteration** | Improve existing products |
| **Trading Up** | Add higher-quality, higher-priced items |
| **Trading Down** | Add lower-priced items to reach more segments |

---

## 🔷 Product Line Decisions

A **Product Line** is a group of closely related products that perform similar functions, are sold to the same customer groups, through the same channels, at similar prices.

| Decision | Description |
|---|---|
| **Line Stretching** | Extending the line upward (premium) or downward (budget) |
| **Line Filling** | Adding more items within the existing price range |
| **Line Pruning** | Dropping weak products from the line |
| **Line Modernisation** | Updating products to keep them current |

# 📌 CHAPTER 9: BRANDING

---

## 🔷 What is a Brand?

A **brand** is a **name, term, sign, symbol, design, or combination** that identifies the products or services of a seller and differentiates them from those of competitors.

> **AMA Definition:** *"A brand is a name, term, sign, symbol, or design or a combination of these intended to identify the goods or services of one seller or group of sellers and to differentiate them from those of competitors."*

### Brand Elements:
| Element | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand Name** | The verbal part of the brand | Coca-Cola, Amul, Tata |
| **Brand Mark / Logo** | The visual symbol or design | Nike swoosh, Apple logo |
| **Trademark** | Brand or part of brand given legal protection | Registered brand names |
| **Trade Name** | Full legal name of the company | Hindustan Unilever Limited |

---

## 🔷 Importance / Advantages of Branding

### For Consumers:
1. **Identification** — Easily identify and locate preferred products
2. **Quality Assurance** — Brand signals consistent quality → reduces purchase risk
3. **Saves Time** — No need to evaluate product each time
4. **Status and Self-Expression** — Premium brands signal status and personality
5. **Trust and Confidence** — Branded products inspire trust

### For Sellers / Companies:
1. **Differentiation** — Distinguishes product from competitors
2. **Premium Pricing** — Strong brands command higher prices
3. **Customer Loyalty** — Brand loyalty generates repeat purchases
4. **Legal Protection** — Trademark protects against copying
5. **Market Segmentation** — Different brands for different segments
6. **Promotional Efficiency** — Strong brand name makes advertising more effective
7. **Facilitates New Product Launch** — Brand extension leverages existing goodwill

---

## 🔷 Brand Equity

**Brand Equity** = The **extra value** a product has because of its brand name — the premium consumers pay and the loyalty they show, compared to a generic product.

**Components of Brand Equity (David Aaker's Model):**
1. **Brand Awareness** — How widely the brand is known
2. **Brand Loyalty** — Degree of repeat purchase and commitment
3. **Perceived Quality** — Consumer's perception of overall quality
4. **Brand Associations** — What the brand makes you think of (values, personality, images)
5. **Other Proprietary Assets** — Patents, trademarks, channel relationships

---

## 🔷 Types of Brands

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Manufacturer / National Brand** | Created and owned by the producer | Levi's, Samsung, Amul |
| **Private Label / Store Brand** | Created by and exclusive to a retailer | Reliance Smart's own-label products |
| **Generic Brand** | No brand name — basic packaging, lowest price | Generic medicines |
| **Family / Umbrella Brand** | One brand name for all products | Tata (Tata Salt, Tata Tea, Tata Motors) |
| **Individual Brand** | Separate brand name for each product | HUL: Dove, Lux, Lifebuoy (separate names) |
| **Co-Brand** | Two brand names on one product | Intel Inside on HP laptops |

---

## 🔷 Branding Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand Extension** | Using existing brand name for new product category | Dettol soap → Dettol hand sanitiser |
| **Line Extension** | New variants within same product line | Colgate Regular → Colgate Sensitive |
| **Multi-branding** | Multiple brands in the same category | HUL: Surf Excel + Rin + Wheel (all detergents) |
| **New Brand** | Entirely new brand for a new product | A company launches a brand new brand name |
| **Co-branding** | Two established brands collaborate | Nike + Apple (Nike+) |

# 📌 CHAPTER 10: PACKAGING, LABELLING AND PRODUCT SUPPORT SERVICES

---

## 🔷 Packaging

**Packaging** is the **process of designing and producing the container or wrapper for a product.** It is sometimes called the "fifth P" of marketing.

> **Philip Kotler:** *"Packaging includes the activities of designing and producing the container or wrapper for a product."*

### Levels of Packaging:
| Level | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Primary Package** | The immediate container holding the product | Toothpaste tube |
| **Secondary Package** | Outer wrapping protecting the primary package | Cardboard box around the tube |
| **Shipping Package** | Container for transportation and storage | Corrugated box of 24 tubes |

---

### Functions / Importance of Packaging:

1. **Protection** — Protects the product from damage, spoilage, contamination during transport and storage
2. **Containment** — Holds the product together for handling
3. **Information** — Communicates product information (ingredients, usage, expiry)
4. **Promotion** — Attractive packaging draws attention at the point of sale — "silent salesman"
5. **Convenience** — Easy-to-open, resealable, portable packaging adds utility
6. **Differentiation** — Distinctive packaging differentiates the product on crowded shelves
7. **Facilitates Storage** — Standardised packaging makes warehousing and stacking efficient
8. **Enables Segmentation** — Different package sizes for different segments (family pack vs single-use)
9. **Brand Building** — Consistent packaging reinforces brand identity
10. **Eco-Positioning** — Sustainable packaging signals environmental responsibility

---

### Characteristics of Good Packaging:
- Attractive and eye-catching
- Protects the product effectively
- Easy to handle, open, and use
- Communicates relevant information clearly
- Economical — cost-effective to produce
- Eco-friendly / recyclable
- Consistent with brand image

---

## 🔷 Labelling

**Labelling** is the **display of information on the product's package** — it is the written, printed, or graphic information on the packaging.

### Types of Labels:
| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Brand Label** | Carries only the brand name | Fruit crates labelled "Amul" |
| **Grade Label** | Identifies quality grade of the product | Grade A, B, C eggs; BIS standards |
| **Descriptive Label** | Gives information about product use, handling, care, ingredients | Most packaged food labels |
| **Informative Label** | Highly detailed information — especially for complex products | Pharmaceutical labels |

### Functions of Labelling:
1. **Identifies** the product and brand
2. **Describes** — ingredients, directions for use, storage, manufacturing and expiry date
3. **Grades** the product quality
4. **Promotes** — attractive label designs serve as promotion
5. **Legal Requirement** — many details (MRP, weight, ingredients) are legally mandatory (Consumer Protection Act, Food Safety Act)
6. **Differentiates** from competing products on the shelf

### Legal Requirements on Labels (India):
- Name and address of manufacturer
- Net weight / volume / quantity
- List of ingredients
- Date of manufacture and expiry
- MRP (Maximum Retail Price) including taxes
- Batch number
- Country of origin (for imports)

---

## 🔷 Product Support Services (After-Sales Services)

**Product Support Services** are services that **supplement physical products** — enhancing value and customer satisfaction after the sale.

### Types of Support Services:
| Service | Description |
|---|---|
| **Installation** | Setting up the product at customer's location |
| **Delivery and Credit** | Home delivery, EMI options |
| **Maintenance and Repair** | Fixing defects after sale |
| **Warranty and Guarantee** | Assurance of product performance; free repair/replacement |
| **Customer Training** | Teaching customers to use complex products |
| **Technical Support / Helpline** | Customer service centres, helplines, online chat |
| **Spare Parts Supply** | Ensuring availability of replacement parts |
| **Upgrade Services** | Software updates, product upgrades |

### Importance of Support Services:
- Increases customer satisfaction and loyalty
- Creates competitive differentiation (two products with similar features — better service wins)
- Reduces post-purchase dissonance
- Builds long-term relationships and repeat business
- Generates additional revenue (extended warranties, service contracts)

# 📌 CHAPTER 11: PRODUCT LIFE CYCLE (PLC)

---

## 🔷 What is the Product Life Cycle?

The **Product Life Cycle (PLC)** describes the **stages a product goes through from its introduction to the market until it is withdrawn.** Like a living organism, a product is born (introduction), grows, matures, and eventually declines.

> **Philip Kotler:** *"The product life cycle is the course of a product's sales and profits over its lifetime."*

---

## 🔷 The 4 Stages of the PLC

```
         SALES &
         PROFITS
            │                    ┌────────────┐
            │                   /              \
            │                  /   MATURITY     \
            │                 /                  \──────────
            │                /  GROWTH             DECLINE
            │               /
            │──────────────/
            │ INTRODUCTION
            │
            └─────────────────────────────────────────→ TIME
```

---

### Stage 1: 🚀 INTRODUCTION

| Aspect | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sales** | Low — product is new and unknown |
| **Profit** | Negative or very low — high launch costs |
| **Customers** | Innovators only (early adopters who try new things) |
| **Competition** | Little or none |
| **Price** | High (skimming) or Low (penetration) |
| **Promotion** | Heavy — building awareness |
| **Distribution** | Selective / Limited |

**Marketing Objective:** Create awareness and encourage trial

**Examples:** Electric vehicles in India in early 2010s; new app launches

---

### Stage 2: 📈 GROWTH

| Aspect | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sales** | Rapidly rising |
| **Profit** | Increasing — costs spread over higher volume |
| **Customers** | Early majority entering the market |
| **Competition** | Increasing — new entrants attracted by profits |
| **Price** | Remains same or slightly reduced |
| **Promotion** | Building preference, not just awareness |
| **Distribution** | Expanding — more outlets |

**Marketing Objective:** Maximise market share

**Examples:** Smartphones in India 2010–2015; OTT platforms 2018–2020

---

### Stage 3: 🏆 MATURITY

| Aspect | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sales** | Peak — growth slows; market saturated |
| **Profit** | Declining — intense price competition |
| **Customers** | Late majority — most potential buyers are using the product |
| **Competition** | Very intense — many competitors |
| **Price** | Under pressure — price wars common |
| **Promotion** | Defensive — maintaining loyalty, differentiating from competitors |
| **Distribution** | Intensive — maximum availability |

**Marketing Objective:** Maximise profit while defending market share

**Strategies to extend maturity:**
- Market Modification — find new users or new uses
- Product Modification — new features, improved quality
- Marketing Mix Modification — new prices, channels, promotions

**Examples:** Toothpaste, soft drinks, colour TV

---

### Stage 4: 📉 DECLINE

| Aspect | Description |
|---|---|
| **Sales** | Declining sharply |
| **Profit** | Very low or negative |
| **Customers** | Laggards — only the most loyal remain |
| **Competition** | Reducing — weak competitors exit |
| **Price** | Low |
| **Promotion** | Minimal — selective |
| **Distribution** | Selective — cutting unprofitable outlets |

**Marketing Objective:** Reduce expenditure and milk the brand

**Strategies:**
- **Maintain** — keep the product for a loyal niche
- **Harvest** — reduce costs, maintain sales and profit as long as possible
- **Drop** — discontinue the product

**Examples:** Landline phones, fax machines, VCDs, black-and-white TVs

---

## 🔷 PLC Summary Table

| Characteristic | Introduction | Growth | Maturity | Decline |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Sales** | Low | Rising fast | Peak, slowing | Declining |
| **Profit** | Negative/Low | Rising | High, then falling | Low/Negative |
| **Customers** | Innovators | Early majority | Late majority | Laggards |
| **Competition** | Low | Growing | Intense | Reducing |
| **Marketing focus** | Awareness | Market share | Defend share | Cut costs |

---

## 🔷 Limitations of PLC

- Not all products follow the same S-shaped curve
- Difficult to know exactly which stage a product is in
- Self-fulfilling prophecy — managers may abandon a product too early, thinking it is in decline
- Different markets may be at different PLC stages for the same product

# 📌 CHAPTER 12: NEW PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT PROCESS (NPD)

---

## 🔷 Why New Products?

- Existing products age and decline
- Customer needs evolve
- Competition forces innovation
- Technology creates new opportunities
- Growth strategy requires new products

> **New Product** includes: Original products, Improved products, Modified products, New brands developed by the firm

---

## 🔷 The 8 Stages of New Product Development

```
STAGE 1 → IDEA GENERATION
         ↓
STAGE 2 → IDEA SCREENING
         ↓
STAGE 3 → CONCEPT DEVELOPMENT & TESTING
         ↓
STAGE 4 → MARKETING STRATEGY DEVELOPMENT
         ↓
STAGE 5 → BUSINESS ANALYSIS
         ↓
STAGE 6 → PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT (Prototype)
         ↓
STAGE 7 → TEST MARKETING
         ↓
STAGE 8 → COMMERCIALISATION (Launch)
```

---

### Stage 1: 💡 Idea Generation
Finding new product ideas from:
- **Internal sources** — R&D department, employees, salespeople
- **External sources** — Customers, competitors, distributors, suppliers, market research

**Techniques:** Brainstorming, customer surveys, competitor analysis, focus groups

---

### Stage 2: 🔍 Idea Screening
Filtering ideas to keep only the promising ones using the **SWOT test** and feasibility check.

**Two types of errors:**
- **Drop error** — Good idea is rejected (costly mistake)
- **Go error** — Poor idea proceeds (even more costly)

Ideas evaluated on: Market potential, company fit, technical feasibility, financial viability

---

### Stage 3: 📋 Concept Development and Testing
Surviving ideas are developed into **product concepts** — detailed descriptions of the idea from the customer's perspective.

- **Product Idea** = A possible product
- **Product Concept** = An elaborated version of the idea in meaningful consumer terms
- **Product Image** = The way consumers perceive the concept

**Concept testing:** Present concept to target consumers and measure reactions — would they buy? How often? At what price?

---

### Stage 4: 📊 Marketing Strategy Development
Develop a preliminary marketing strategy for the new product:
- **Part 1:** Target market, planned positioning, sales/market share/profit goals
- **Part 2:** Planned price, distribution, and marketing budget
- **Part 3:** Long-term sales, profit goals, and marketing mix strategy

---

### Stage 5: 💰 Business Analysis
Evaluate the business attractiveness of the proposal:
- Estimate sales volumes and revenue
- Estimate costs (R&D, production, marketing)
- Calculate break-even point and projected profit
- **If financials look good → move to product development**

---

### Stage 6: 🔧 Product Development (Prototype)
The R&D or engineering team develops the product concept into a **physical product (prototype):**
- Functional prototype built and tested
- Consumer testing of the prototype
- Technical refinements based on feedback

**Most expensive stage** — if the prototype fails, huge investment is lost

---

### Stage 7: 🧪 Test Marketing
The product is **launched in one or a few representative markets** to test the full marketing programme:
- Real sales data collected
- Consumer response measured
- Marketing mix (price, promotion, distribution) fine-tuned

**Types:**
- **Standard test market** — actual city rollout
- **Controlled test market** — selected stores managed by a research firm
- **Simulated test market** — laboratory shopping environment

**Advantage:** Reduces risk before full national launch
**Disadvantage:** Time-consuming, expensive, alerts competitors

---

### Stage 8: 🚀 Commercialisation (Full Launch)
Full-scale production and national/international market launch:
- **WHEN to launch** — timing (avoid competitor launches, choose right season)
- **WHERE to launch** — geographic rollout strategy (city by city, region by region, or national)
- **TO WHOM** — target early adopters and heavy users first
- **HOW to launch** — action plan with advertising, distribution, sales force deployment

---

## 🔷 Reasons for New Product Failure

| Reason | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Market too small** | Overestimated market size |
| **Poor product** | Did not perform as promised |
| **Ineffective positioning** | Wrong positioning or poor targeting |
| **Incorrect pricing** | Too expensive or too cheap |
| **Poor distribution** | Product not available where needed |
| **Strong competition** | Competitor retaliated strongly |
| **Poor timing** | Launched at the wrong time |
| **High development cost** | Ate into profits |

# 📌 PART 3: PRICING OF PRODUCT

---

## 🔷 What is Price?

**Price** is the **amount of money a buyer must pay to obtain a product or service.** It is the only element of the marketing mix that **generates revenue** — all other elements generate costs.

> **Philip Kotler:** *"Price is the amount of money charged for a product or service, or the sum of the values that consumers exchange for the benefits of having or using the product."*

---

## 🔷 Significance / Importance of Pricing

1. **Only Revenue-Generating Element** — Price directly determines revenue and profitability
2. **Flexible Marketing Tool** — Price can be changed faster than product or distribution
3. **Communicates Value and Positioning** — High price signals premium quality; low price signals value
4. **Competitive Weapon** — Price cuts can gain market share; price premiums signal superiority
5. **Affects Demand** — Price changes directly affect sales volume (Law of Demand)
6. **Determines Market Position** — Sets the product's price-quality image in consumers' minds
7. **Survival** — In tough times, reducing price may be critical for business survival
8. **Influences Distribution** — Channel members' margins are determined by pricing
9. **Impacts Brand Equity** — Consistent premium pricing builds and reinforces brand equity
10. **Regulatory / Social Significance** — Pricing has social implications — fair pricing matters to society and government

---

## 🔷 Factors Affecting Pricing Decisions

### 🅰️ Internal Factors (Controllable)

| Factor | How it Affects Pricing |
|---|---|
| **Cost of Production** | Price must cover costs — sets the floor (minimum price) |
| **Marketing Objectives** | Survival → low price; Market share → penetration price; Profit max → high price |
| **Marketing Mix** | Premium product + luxury distribution → high price expected |
| **Product Differentiation** | Unique products command higher prices |
| **Company Reputation** | Strong brand allows premium pricing |
| **Organisational Considerations** | Who has pricing authority — management, sales, marketing? |

### 🅱️ External Factors (Less Controllable)

| Factor | How it Affects Pricing |
|---|---|
| **Demand** | High demand allows higher pricing; elastic demand limits price increases |
| **Competition** | In competitive markets, price is constrained by rivals |
| **Economic Conditions** | Recession → consumers price-sensitive → pressure to lower prices |
| **Government Regulations** | Price controls, anti-dumping laws, MRP regulations |
| **Consumer Perception** | Perceived value sets the ceiling (maximum price) |
| **Distribution Channel** | Intermediary margins must be included in price |
| **Social Considerations** | Essential goods — pricing must consider public welfare |

---

## 🔷 Pricing Objectives

| Objective | Strategy |
|---|---|
| **Survival** | Set price to cover costs and survive tough times |
| **Maximum Current Profit** | Set price that maximises short-term profit |
| **Maximum Market Share** | Set low price to gain market share (penetration) |
| **Market Skimming** | Set high initial price to skim maximum revenue from early adopters |
| **Product Quality Leadership** | Premium price to signal and sustain high quality positioning |
| **Social Pricing** | Keep prices low for social benefit (essential medicines, public services) |

---

## 🔷 Pricing Methods / Approaches

### 🅰️ Cost-Based Pricing

**1. Cost-Plus Pricing (Mark-Up Pricing)**
Add a standard percentage mark-up to the cost of the product.

```
Selling Price = Cost + (Mark-up % × Cost)
Example: Cost = ₹100, Mark-up = 30%
Selling Price = ₹100 + ₹30 = ₹130
```

**Advantages:** Simple; ensures cost recovery; widely used
**Disadvantages:** Ignores demand and competition; may not reflect true value

**2. Break-Even Pricing (Target Return Pricing)**
Set price to break even or achieve a target rate of return on investment.

```
Break-Even Volume = Fixed Costs ÷ (Price − Variable Cost per unit)
```

---

### 🅱️ Value-Based Pricing
Set price based on **buyers' perception of value** — not on seller's cost.

- Start with the **customer's value perception**, then build the product and cost structure to deliver that value at the target price
- Used by premium and luxury brands

```
Customer Value Perception → Price → Cost → Product
(opposite of cost-based pricing)
```

---

### 🅲 Competition-Based Pricing
Set price **based on what competitors charge:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Going-Rate Pricing** | Price at same level as major competitors |
| **Competitive Bidding** | Price to win contracts — common in B2B |
| **Below-Competitor** | Price lower to gain market share |
| **Above-Competitor** | Price higher to signal premium quality |

---

### 🅳 Demand-Based Pricing
Set price based on the **level and elasticity of demand:**
- High demand → higher price
- Elastic demand → price cuts boost volume significantly
- Inelastic demand → price increases don't reduce demand much

---

## 🔷 Pricing Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Best Used When | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Market Skimming** | Start high; lower over time | Product is innovative; early adopters will pay premium | New iPhone; new tech gadgets |
| **Penetration Pricing** | Start low to quickly gain market share | Price-sensitive market; want rapid adoption | Jio's ₹0 launch; new FMCG brand |
| **Psychological Pricing** | Price to affect perception | Consumer psychology drives purchase | ₹999 instead of ₹1000 |
| **Premium / Prestige Pricing** | Permanently high price to signal quality | Luxury positioning | Rolex; Mercedes; Chanel |
| **Economy Pricing** | Bare minimum price; low cost, low price | Cost-conscious segment | Generic medicines; budget airlines |
| **Bundle Pricing** | Multiple products sold together at one price | To increase volume and perceived value | Combo meals; software suites |
| **Promotional Pricing** | Temporarily reduce price to stimulate sales | Short-term sales boost | Festival offers; end-of-season sale |
| **Geographic Pricing** | Different prices in different locations | Different costs or market conditions | FOB pricing; Zone pricing |
| **Discriminatory Pricing** | Different prices for different segments | Segments have different willingness to pay | Movie: student ticket vs normal ticket |
| **Product Line Pricing** | Different price points within a product line | Multiple variants targeting different segments | Economy, Standard, Premium variants |

---

## 🔷 Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

**Price Elasticity** measures **how sensitive demand is to a change in price.**

```
PED = % Change in Quantity Demanded
      ─────────────────────────────
         % Change in Price
```

| PED Value | Type | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| PED > 1 | Elastic | Price increase → significant fall in demand (e.g., luxury goods, optional items) |
| PED < 1 | Inelastic | Price increase → small fall in demand (e.g., essential goods, medicines) |
| PED = 1 | Unit elastic | % change in price = % change in demand |

**Marketing Implication:**
- Elastic demand → lower price can increase total revenue
- Inelastic demand → higher price increases total revenue

---

## 🔷 Price Change Strategies

### Initiating Price Cuts (Reasons):
- Excess capacity
- Declining market share
- Drive out competition (predatory pricing)

### Initiating Price Increases (Reasons):
- Cost inflation
- Excess demand
- Improve profitability

### Responding to Competitor Price Changes:
- Maintain price (if differentiation strong enough)
- Reduce price (if market is price-sensitive)
- Raise perceived quality (promotional response)
- Launch a low-price fighter brand

# 📌 PART 4: MANAGEMENT OF DISTRIBUTION (PLACE)

---

## 🔷 What is Distribution / Place?

**Distribution (Place)** refers to all the **activities involved in making a product available to target customers** — at the right place, at the right time, in the right quantity.

> **Philip Kotler:** *"A distribution channel is a set of interdependent organisations that help make a product or service available for use or consumption by the consumer or business user."*

A **marketing channel** (or **distribution channel**) is the path taken by goods and services as they move from **producer to consumer.**

---

## 🔷 Importance of Distribution

1. **Bridges Gap Between Producers and Consumers** — Manufacturers and consumers are geographically separated; channels bridge this gap
2. **Creates Time Utility** — Makes products available when consumers want them
3. **Creates Place Utility** — Makes products available where consumers want them
4. **Creates Possession Utility** — Facilitates transfer of ownership
5. **Reduces Transactions** — 3 producers and 3 consumers = 9 transactions without intermediaries; with 1 intermediary = 6 transactions
6. **Provides Market Information** — Channel members pass valuable market and customer information back to producers
7. **Promotional Support** — Retailers display and promote products at the point of sale
8. **After-Sales Service** — Dealers provide installation, maintenance, and warranty services

---

## 🔷 Distribution Channel Levels

```
LEVEL 0 (DIRECT):
Producer ────────────────────────────────────→ Consumer

LEVEL 1:
Producer ──────────→ Retailer ───────────────→ Consumer

LEVEL 2:
Producer → Wholesaler → Retailer ────────────→ Consumer

LEVEL 3:
Producer → Agent → Wholesaler → Retailer ────→ Consumer
```

**Direct Channel (Level 0):** Used when customers are few, geographically concentrated, or purchases are large and complex (B2B machinery, insurance, D2C e-commerce brands)

**Indirect Channels:** Used when consumers are widely dispersed, frequent purchases of low-value goods, wide geographic coverage needed

---

## 🔷 Channel Members / Marketing Intermediaries

### 1. 🏭 Wholesalers
Buy goods in **large quantities from manufacturers** and resell in smaller quantities to retailers.

**Functions of Wholesalers:**
- Bulk breaking (buying large, selling small)
- Warehousing and storage
- Transportation
- Financing (credit to retailers)
- Risk bearing (holding inventory)
- Market information
- Promotion

**Types of Wholesalers:**
| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Merchant Wholesalers** | Buy and own goods; bear risk |
| **Agents and Brokers** | Don't own goods; earn commission |
| **Manufacturer's Sales Branch** | Company-owned wholesale operation |

---

### 2. 🛒 Retailers
Buy goods from wholesalers (or manufacturers) and sell **directly to end consumers.**

**Functions of Retailers:**
- Providing assortment (variety of products in one place)
- Breaking bulk (selling in small quantities)
- Holding inventory
- Providing services (credit, delivery, advice)
- Point-of-sale promotion

**Types of Retailers:**

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Departmental Stores** | Large stores with many departments | Shoppers Stop, Lifestyle |
| **Supermarkets / Hypermarkets** | Large, self-service, low-cost | Big Bazaar, DMart |
| **Specialty Stores** | Focus on one product category | Tanishq (jewellery), Croma (electronics) |
| **Convenience Stores** | Small, nearby, long hours | Local kirana shops |
| **Discount Stores** | High volume, low prices | Costco, Price Club |
| **Catalogue / Online Retailing** | Orders by catalogue or internet | Amazon, Flipkart |
| **Superstore** | Extremely large — food + non-food | Walmart Supercenter |

---

## 🔷 Types of Distribution (Coverage Strategies)

| Type | Description | When Used | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Intensive Distribution** | Product available in as many outlets as possible | Convenience goods — maximum reach needed | Coca-Cola, Maggi, Colgate |
| **Selective Distribution** | Product available in selected, carefully chosen outlets | Shopping goods — some intermediary control needed | LG, Samsung TVs in authorised stores |
| **Exclusive Distribution** | Product available at only one or very few outlets per region | Specialty/luxury goods — tight control over brand image | Rolex watches, Rolls-Royce cars, major appliance brands |

---

## 🔷 Channel Design Decisions

**Step 1: Analyse Customer Needs**
- What lot sizes do customers want?
- How much waiting time is acceptable?
- What breadth of assortment do they want?
- Do they want additional services?

**Step 2: Set Channel Objectives**
- Target segment's service needs
- Cost constraints
- Legal and competitive environment

**Step 3: Identify Major Channel Alternatives**
- Types of intermediaries (agents, distributors, retailers, franchises)
- Number of intermediaries (intensive, selective, exclusive)

**Step 4: Evaluate the Alternatives**
- Economic criteria (which gives best sales and costs?)
- Control criteria (which allows best control over how product is presented?)
- Adaptive criteria (which is most flexible?)

---

## 🔷 Channel Management Decisions

After designing the channel, the company must:
1. **Select channel members** — evaluate their financial strength, reputation, coverage, compatibility
2. **Motivate channel members** — margins, co-op advertising, training, performance bonuses
3. **Evaluate channel members** — regular performance reviews against sales targets
4. **Modify channel arrangements** — add, drop, or change intermediaries as market evolves

---

## 🔷 Physical Distribution / Logistics Management

**Physical Distribution (Marketing Logistics)** = The activities involved in the **physical movement and storage of goods** from producer to consumer.

### Key Logistics Activities:

| Activity | Description |
|---|---|
| **Order Processing** | Receiving and fulfilling customer orders efficiently |
| **Warehousing** | Storing goods until needed — deciding location and number of warehouses |
| **Inventory Management** | How much stock to hold — balance between stockouts and holding costs |
| **Transportation** | Moving goods from producer to channel members to customers |
| **Materials Handling** | Physical handling of goods in warehouses |

### Modes of Transportation:

| Mode | Best For | Advantage | Disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Road (Trucks)** | Short-to-medium distance, diverse goods | Flexible, door-to-door | Slower for long distance |
| **Rail** | Bulk, heavy, long-distance goods | Low cost for bulk | Not door-to-door |
| **Air** | High-value, perishable, urgent | Fastest | Most expensive |
| **Water (Ship)** | Bulk, heavy international goods | Cheapest for large volumes | Very slow |
| **Pipeline** | Liquids and gases (oil, gas) | Continuous, low cost | Very limited applicability |

---

## 🔷 Modern Distribution Concepts

### E-Commerce / Online Distribution
- Digital channels eliminate several layers of intermediaries
- Direct-to-consumer (D2C) model growing rapidly
- Examples: Amazon, Flipkart, company's own websites

### Multi-Channel and Omni-Channel Distribution
- **Multi-channel:** Using several separate channels (store + website + app)
- **Omni-channel:** Fully integrated customer experience across all channels — customer can browse online, buy in-store, return via app

### Franchising
A form of distribution where the **franchisee** gets the right to use the franchisor's brand, business model, and support in exchange for a fee.
- McDonald's, Domino's, DTDC courier — franchise-based distribution

# 📌 PART 5: PROMOTION (TILL IMPORTANCE OF PROMOTION)

---

## 🔷 What is Promotion?

**Promotion** refers to all the **communication activities** used by a marketer to inform, persuade, and remind target customers about the organisation's products and services — with the ultimate goal of influencing buying behaviour.

Promotion is the **"voice" of the marketing mix** — it communicates the product's value proposition to the target market.

> **Philip Kotler:** *"Promotion encompasses all the tools in the marketing mix whose major role is persuasive communication."*

> **William Stanton:** *"Promotion is the element in an organisation's marketing mix that serves to inform, persuade and remind the market of the organisation and/or its products."*

---

## 🔷 The Promotion Mix (Marketing Communications Mix)

The **promotion mix** is the specific combination of promotional tools a company uses:

| Tool | Description |
|---|---|
| **Advertising** | Paid, non-personal mass communication via media |
| **Sales Promotion** | Short-term incentives to encourage purchase |
| **Personal Selling** | Direct face-to-face selling by sales representatives |
| **Public Relations (PR)** | Building positive image through unpaid media and community activities |
| **Direct Marketing** | Direct communication with targeted individuals (email, SMS, telemarketing) |
| **Digital / Online Marketing** | Internet, social media, content marketing, SEO, influencer marketing |

---

## 🔷 Objectives of Promotion

| Objective | Description |
|---|---|
| **Inform** | Create awareness about a new product, price change, or service |
| **Persuade** | Convince customers to prefer and purchase the brand |
| **Remind** | Remind existing customers to repurchase; keep brand top-of-mind |
| **Reinforce** | Reassure existing customers that they made the right choice |
| **Differentiate** | Distinguish the product from competing brands |
| **Build Brand Image** | Create a strong, positive brand identity and reputation |

---

## 🔷 Importance of Promotion

**1. Creates Awareness**
In a crowded marketplace, customers cannot buy what they don't know exists. Promotion is the primary tool for **creating awareness** about new products, services, features, or brands.

> Without promotion, even the best product may fail because no one knows about it.

**2. Informs and Educates Customers**
Promotion **communicates product information** — features, benefits, how to use, where to buy, price — giving customers the knowledge to make informed decisions.

**3. Persuades and Influences Purchase**
Promotion goes beyond information to **persuade** customers to prefer one brand over another and take the action of buying.

**4. Builds and Maintains Brand Image**
Consistent, strategic promotion **builds a strong brand image** — creating trust, recognition, and emotional connection that gives the brand long-term competitive strength.

**5. Generates Demand**
Effective promotion **stimulates demand** — both primary demand (for the product category) and selective demand (for the specific brand).

**6. Increases Sales and Market Share**
By reaching more customers, persuading more of them to buy, and encouraging repeat purchases — promotion **directly drives sales volume and market share.**

**7. Supports Competitive Strategy**
In competitive markets, promotion is a key **competitive weapon** — helping the company retain customers, attract competitor's customers, and respond to competitive moves.

**8. Facilitates Introduction of New Products**
New products have zero awareness. Promotion is **essential for launching new products** — building awareness, trial, and adoption among target customers.

**9. Helps in Price Justification**
Strong promotion that communicates unique benefits helps companies **justify premium prices** — customers pay more when they understand what makes a product worth more.

**10. Strengthens Distribution**
Promotions that create consumer pull encourage **retailers and distributors** to stock the product — promotion supports the entire distribution channel.

**11. Retains Existing Customers**
Reminder advertising and loyalty promotions **retain existing customers** — often more cost-efficient than acquiring new ones.

**12. Educates Society**
Social marketing and public service promotions (anti-tobacco, road safety, health campaigns) **educate society** and change behaviour for public good.

**13. Stabilises Sales**
Promotional activity during slow periods (off-season offers, festival promotions) helps **smooth out demand fluctuations** and stabilise sales throughout the year.

**14. Creates Goodwill**
PR activities and cause-related marketing promotions **build goodwill** and a positive reputation — which is a long-term business asset.

---

## 🔷 The Communication Process in Promotion

Effective promotion requires understanding how **communication works:**

```
SENDER (Marketer)
      ↓
ENCODING (Converting idea into words/images/symbols)
      ↓
MESSAGE (The actual advertisement/promotion)
      ↓
MEDIA (Channel: TV, newspaper, social media, salesperson)
      ↓
DECODING (Receiver interprets the message)
      ↓
RECEIVER (Customer/Target Audience)
      ↓
RESPONSE (Attitude, interest, purchase behaviour)
      ↓
FEEDBACK (Sales data, customer research, response rates)
      ↑
NOISE (Competing messages, distractions, misunderstandings)
```

**Key challenges:**
- **Noise** — competing messages and distractions prevent the message from being received clearly
- **Selective attention** — consumers screen out most marketing messages
- **Perceptual distortion** — consumers interpret messages through their own filters

---

## 🔷 The AIDA Model of Promotion

The **AIDA model** describes the stages a customer goes through in response to promotion:

```
A → ATTENTION
    (Capture the customer's attention in a cluttered market)
         ↓
I → INTEREST
    (Generate interest by showing relevant benefits)
         ↓
D → DESIRE
    (Create desire — make the customer WANT the product)
         ↓
A → ACTION
    (Drive action — purchase, visit website, call for more info)
```

**Extended Model — AIDCA:**
- A → Attention
- I → Interest
- D → Desire
- C → **Conviction** (remove doubt, build confidence)
- A → Action

---

## 🔷 Factors Affecting the Promotion Mix

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Type of Product** | Consumer goods → more advertising; Industrial goods → more personal selling |
| **Target Market** | Reach, literacy, media habits of the target audience determine tools |
| **Stage of PLC** | Introduction → heavy advertising; Maturity → more sales promotion |
| **Budget Available** | Small budget → cannot afford mass TV advertising; must use cost-effective tools |
| **Competitor Promotions** | Must respond to or differentiate from what competitors are doing |
| **Push vs Pull Strategy** | Push → focus on intermediaries (personal selling, trade promotions); Pull → focus on consumers (advertising) |
| **Nature of Market** | B2B markets → personal selling dominant; B2C → advertising dominant |

---

## 🔷 Push vs Pull Strategy

| Aspect | PUSH Strategy | PULL Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Direction** | Producer → Intermediaries → Consumer | Consumer ← Producer (demand flows back) |
| **Focus** | Motivate channel members to stock and sell | Motivate end consumers to demand the product |
| **Tools** | Trade discounts, personal selling to dealers, dealer contests | Consumer advertising, sales promotions, social media |
| **Best for** | Industrial goods, new products with limited brand awareness | Strong consumer brands with loyal customers |
| **Example** | Pharmaceutical company promoting to doctors | Nike's consumer advertising creating demand |
